Sephardi-Ladino Food Culture

Lesson 376 · אוֹכֶל סְפָרַדִּי-לָאדִינוֹ
Home376

From the kitchens of Salonika, Istanbul, Izmir, Sarajevo and Sofia, the Sephardi-Ladino culinary world traveled with the expelled families of 1492 across the Ottoman Empire and eventually into Israel. Bourekas at every street corner, huevos haminados at every Pesach Seder, biscochos beside the morning coffee — what looks like generic Israeli food is often Ladino food that quietly took over. This lesson teaches the vocabulary of that hidden inheritance.

Cultural note — Bourekas are so embedded in Israeli life that they have a code: a triangle is cheese, a square is potato, a round one is mushroom, a long one is spinach. The shape language was set by Sephardi bakers in the 1950s and is still respected. If you walk into a bourekas-shop and ask for "the cheese one" by shape, you have already passed the first cultural test.
