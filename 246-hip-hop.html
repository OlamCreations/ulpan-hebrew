Israeli Hip-Hop — From Dudu Faruk to Tuna

Lesson 246 · הִיפ-הוֹפ יִשְׂרָאֵלִי
Home246

Israeli hip-hop emerged in the late 1990s with Subliminal (Kobi Shimoni) and HaTzel (The Shadow, Yoav Eliasi), whose rivalry became the genre's first defining beef. Today the scene is far more diverse: Tuna (Hen Shimoni-Cohen) is the lyricist's lyricist, Dudu Faruk weaponizes satire against politics and culture, Stephane Legar fuses Mizrahi pop with rap, and Static & Ben El Tavori reign over mainstream pop-rap charts. Other major voices include Peled, Eden Hason, and Margi. Stylistically, Hebrew rap mixes Hebrew rhyme with Arabic and English borrowings, splintering into trap, Mizrahi-rap fusion, and satirical streams.

Cultural note — Hebrew rap is technically demanding: Hebrew verbs change form by gender and tense, making rhyme schemes harder than in English. Tuna is widely recognized as one of the most lyrically sophisticated rappers, while Dudu Faruk's satirical style mocks Israeli politics and culture. Static & Ben El Tavori sit closer to pop chart territory than to purist hip-hop. Many hip-hop artists collaborate with Mizrahi singers (Eyal Golan and Sarit Hadad have featured rapper guests). In Tel Aviv, look for Habima Square plaza concerts, Bloomfield Stadium for big tours, and Barby for hip-hop nights.
