Mental Health — Briut Nafshit in Israel

Lesson 225 · בְּרִיאוּת נַפְשִׁית
Home225

Israeli mental health coverage was reformed in 2015 — psychotherapy and psychiatry are now covered by the kupot cholim, with roughly 30 sessions per year subsidized. The cultural shift accelerated post-October 7 (~2023): about one in three Israelis sought some form of mental health support after the war. Common diagnoses include chrida (anxiety), dikaon (depression), and PTSD (post-traumatic stress, often tied to military service or terror exposure). The dominant approach combines CBT (Western), EMDR (trauma-focused), and growing acceptance of medication.

Cultural note — Mental health stigma in Israel has shrunk dramatically post-2023. Public figures, ex-soldiers, and government officials openly discuss therapy. The IDF runs its own mental health corps; military trauma is treated separately by Mivtzanim and Brit Olam programs. For olim, English-speaking therapists are most available in Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, and Modi\'in. Wait times for psychiatry can be 2-4 months at the kupot, so many Israelis pay private (~600-800 NIS/session). Suicide hotline: ERAN (1201).
