Hebrew Poetry

Lesson 195 · מְשׁוֹרְרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל
Home195

Hebrew poetry has a 3000-year continuum from the Psalms to slam poetry on Tel Aviv stages. Modern Hebrew poetry began in the 1890s with Chaim Nachman Bialik, who reanimated the language for secular literature. Yehuda Amichai (1924-2000) is the most translated Israeli poet, his lines quoted at funerals, weddings, and Knesset speeches. Naomi Shemer wrote "Yerushalayim shel Zahav" — the unofficial second national anthem, sung at every public ceremony. Spoken-word and slam poetry thrives in Tel Aviv venues, and reading poetry deepens vocabulary and the older emotional register of Hebrew.

Cultural note — Poetry has a special status in Israeli culture rare in the modern world. Politicians quote Bialik to settle arguments, school children memorize Amichai for graduation, soldiers write home in poems and friends read those poems aloud at memorials. The emotional register of poetry is older, biblical, mystical — modern Israeli poetry blends biblical Hebrew with everyday speech in ways prose cannot. Yom HaShoah and Yom HaZikaron use specific liturgical poems read on radio; many poems set to music become national hits, sung by everyone.
