Hanukkah — The Festival of Lights

חֲנֻכָּה · 8 NIGHTS · 25 KISLEV → 2/3 TEVET · NES GADOL HAYA SHAM
Home Hanukkah
Hanukkah commemorates the victory of the Maccabees over the Seleucid Greeks (167-160 BCE) and the subsequent rededication of the Second Temple in Jerusalem (164 BCE). Eight nights, starting 25 Kislev (December). The central rite: lighting the hanukkiyah (8-branched lamp + 1 servant candle, the shamash). One candle the first night, two the second, and so on — adding rather than diminishing. Foods fried in oil: sufganiyot (jelly donuts), levivot/latkes (potato pancakes). Children play dreidel (sevivon) and receive Hanukkah gelt (small coins or chocolate coins). Not a biblically commanded holiday — work is permitted — but loved across the religious spectrum.

The famous Talmudic passage (Shabbat 21b) — the only canonical Jewish text that mentions the oil miracle (the books of Maccabees are not part of the Hebrew Bible). The miracle of nes pach hashemen is the rabbinic interpretive frame for the holiday. The military victory is mentioned in Al HaNissim in the Amida, but the home rite focuses on light.

♦ About Hanukkah

The most public Jewish holiday — designed to be seen. Hanukkah candles must be placed where they can be seen from outside (a window or doorway), to publicize the miracle (pirsumei nisa). In Jerusalem and other Israeli cities, hanukkiyot light up every window. Public lightings at major squares, IDF bases, government buildings.

How to light

Light starts on the right; each subsequent night a candle is added to the left. The newest candle is lit first, then moving rightward. The shamash (servant candle) is lit first and used to kindle the others — never the other candles to light each other. Three blessings the first night (lehadlik, she'asa nissim, shehecheyanu); two on subsequent nights. Lit at nightfall or earlier on Friday (before Shabbat begins). Must stay lit at least 30 minutes after nightfall.

The historical victory

168 BCE: Antiochus IV outlawed Jewish practice (circumcision, Shabbat, kosher laws), placed a statue of Zeus in the Temple, ordered pigs sacrificed there. The Maccabean revolt was sparked by the priest Mattathias killing a Jew who agreed to sacrifice to the Greek gods. His five sons led the guerilla war. After 3 years they recaptured Jerusalem; rededicated the Temple 25 Kislev 164 BCE. They ruled Judea as Hasmoneans for the next 100+ years. The story is told in 1 & 2 Maccabees (Apocrypha, not in the Hebrew Bible — accepted by Catholic but not Jewish or Protestant canon).

Why not in the Tanakh?

Because the Maccabean books were written too late (around 100 BCE) for the canonical Jewish biblical canon (closed by the time of the Mishnah, ~200 CE). Also: the later Hasmonean rulers behaved badly (corrupt, civil war, alliance with Rome), souring the rabbis on celebrating the family. The Talmud preserves the holiday but shifts the focus from military glory to the miracle of the oil — a quieter, theological frame.

Sufganiyot in Israel

Bakeries start selling sufganiyot from late November. By Hanukkah every cafe and bakery has 5+ varieties (classic strawberry jam, dulce de leche, halva, chocolate, custard, pistachio, etc.). Israeli food media tracks new flavors every year. The standard sufganiya = ~400 calories — Hanukkah weight gain is a national joke.

Dreidel: the four letters

נ (Nun) — nichts (nothing, Yiddish): get nothing. ג (Gimel) — gantz (whole): take the whole pot. ה (Heh) — halb (half): take half. ש (Shin) — shtel (put in): put one in. In Israel the last letter is פ (Peh) for po (here) — 'a great miracle happened HERE,' since you're in Israel.
