Hebrew Calendar: The Lunisolar Year

לוּחַ הַשָּׁנָה הָעִבְרִי · 12 LUNAR MONTHS · 7 LEAP YEARS PER 19 · STRUCTURE OF JEWISH TIME
Home Hebrew Calendar
The Hebrew calendar is lunisolar: months follow the moon (29 or 30 days, beginning at the new moon, Rosh Chodesh), but the year follows the sun, with a 13th month (Adar II) inserted seven times every 19 years to keep Pesach in the spring as the Torah commands (Deut 16:1). Each month has a name borrowed from Babylonian during the Babylonian exile (6th c. BCE). The new year starts twice: Rosh HaShana on 1 Tishrei (civil), and 1 Nisan (the religious / Exodus calendar). Days run from sunset to sunset, not midnight to midnight, the biblical pattern from Genesis 1: "and there was evening, and there was morning."

♦ About the Hebrew calendar

A lunisolar calendar continuously used for ~3,500 years. The current fixed arithmetic version was codified by Hillel II in 359 CE after observation-based sighting of the new moon became impractical for the dispersed Jewish world. The year 2026 is Hebrew year 5786-5787 (transition on Rosh HaShana, autumn 2026).

Why lunisolar?

The moon gives the months (Rosh Chodesh = literal sighting of the crescent in ancient times). The sun gives the year (Pesach must fall in aviv, Deut 16:1, the spring barley-ripening season). Both must align, hence the 7 leap years per 19-year Metonic cycle, named after the Greek astronomer Meton (5th c. BCE) but used in Babylonian astronomy and adopted by the rabbis.

Why does Tishrei come 'first' but Nisan is month 1?

The Torah counts months from Nisan (Ex 12:2). But four 'new years' exist in rabbinic tradition (Mishna Rosh HaShana 1:1): 1 Tishrei (civil + agricultural + judgment of nations), 1 Nisan (kings + festivals), 1 Elul (tithing of animals), 15 Shevat (Tu BiShvat, trees). Tishrei became the 'main' new year because creation is traditionally dated to 25 Elul, with Adam created on 1 Tishrei.

Days of the week

Yom Rishon (Sunday) → Yom Sheni (Monday) → Yom Shlishi, Revii, Chamishi, Shishi. Saturday is not 'Yom Shvi'i', it's Shabbat, named not numbered. The other six days are counted in relation to it. Six days of work, one day of rest; the week is the Torah's most original chronological unit.

When the calendar resets

Each Hebrew year has a key date: Rosh HaShana 1 Tishrei shifts each year on the Gregorian calendar (Sept or Oct). 2026: Friday 11 September. 2027: Saturday 2 October. 2028: Thursday 21 September. The Hebrew year number always advances on the Hebrew new year, not Jan 1.
