Moroccan Jewish music is one of the deepest living traditions in the Sephardi world. It carries Al-Andalus across nine centuries — the courtly suites of Cordoba and Granada, kept alive by Jewish musicians who refused to let the music die when the Catholic Monarchs expelled both Jews and Moors in 1492. In Fez, Meknes, Tetouan and Essaouira, those same maqamat became the soundtrack of Shabbat tables, hilula pilgrimages, and pre-dawn vigils. Today they fill stadiums in Ashdod and Beersheva. This lesson maps the lineage from the Al-Ala suite to Eyal Golan.

Cultural note — When you hear an Israeli call something "muzika mizrachit" (Eastern music), they often mean specifically the Moroccan-Andalusian-Yemenite-Iraqi synthesis that exploded into the mainstream in the 1980s, after decades of being dismissed as "wedding music". The 2024 Andalusian Orchestra of Ashkelon plays the same nubas Maimonides would have heard in Fez. The bakashot vigils still run at Ohel Moshe, Adess and Bat Yam every Friday night from late October to mid-January (Vayetze through Vayechi). If you arrive in Israel with even basic recognition of the Al-Ala repertoire, Moroccan-Israeli families will treat you like family within minutes.
