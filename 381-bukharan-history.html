Bukharan Jewry — Origins and Central Asian History

Lesson 381 · יַהֲדוּת בּוּכָרָה · מָקוֹר וְהִיסְטוֹרְיָה
Home381

Block 39 opens with one of the oldest and most isolated Jewish communities on earth. The Bukharan Jews trace 2,500 years of presence in Central Asia — claimed descendants of the Babylonian exile who moved eastward along the Silk Road and rooted in the oasis cities of Bukhara, Samarkand, Tashkent, Dushanbe, Khujand. They spoke Bukhori, a Judeo-Tajik dialect of Persian written in Hebrew letters. They were dhimmis under the Emirate of Bukhara, wore yellow patches, were forbidden to ride horses inside city walls, lived in low-roofed homes. The 16th-century kabbalist Yusuf Maman traveled from Tzfat to revive their learning. The 1868 Russian conquest brought relative reform. The first wave of aliyah from 1868 to 1917 founded the Bukharan Quarter of Jerusalem (Shchunat HaBucharim, 1891). This is the opening lesson on a community most Israelis only half-know.

Cultural note — When Israelis say "Bucharim" they almost always mean the Jerusalem neighborhood — the grand 19th-century quarter built by wealthy Bukharan merchants, with mansions still bearing their names (Davidoff, Moussaieff, Hefetz). The actual community is small (~150,000 worldwide, ~50,000 in Israel) but its visibility is disproportionate. Bukharan Jews are distinct from Persian Jews despite a shared Persian linguistic substrate — different liturgy (Edot HaMizrach with Bukharan inflections), different cuisine (plov as the central dish, not chelo kebab), different musical tradition (shashmaqam, the Central Asian classical mode-cycle). Knowing this distinction is a sign of cultural literacy in Israel.
