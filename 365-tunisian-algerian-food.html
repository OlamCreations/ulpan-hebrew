Tunisian-Algerian Jewish Food

Lesson 365 · אֹכֶל יְהוּדִי תּוּנִיסַאי-אַלְגֵ'ירַאי
Home365

Tunisian and Algerian Jews — the "Maghreb de l'Est" — brought to Israel a cuisine sharper, hotter, and more sea-driven than the Moroccan one. Brik with its molten egg yolk, fricassé sold on every Tunisian-Israeli street, ojja hissing in cast iron, pkaila simmering Friday night — these are the foundations. This lesson covers the dishes, the sauces, the Shabbat stews, and the spirits, with the Hebrew you will hear in any Tunisian-Algerian kitchen in Israel.

Cultural note — Tunisian and Algerian Jews left mostly between 1948 and 1967. They settled heavily in Beersheba, Ashdod, Netivot, Dimona, Sderot. Their food is now part of mainstream Israeli cuisine — shakshuka and harissa are everywhere — but the original Tunisian-Jewish dishes (brik, fricassé, pkaila, mahia) remain proudly community-specific. Order brik in a Tunisian-Israeli restaurant and you signal you know what you are doing.
