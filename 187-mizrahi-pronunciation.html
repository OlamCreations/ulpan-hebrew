Mizrahi-Arabic Pronunciation Heritage

Lesson 187 · עִבְרִית מִזְרָחִית
Home187

Mizrahi Jews — those who came from Arab and Muslim lands (Iraq, Yemen, Morocco, Iran, Tunisia, Egypt) — preserved distinct Hebrew pronunciation features that link directly to ancient Hebrew and Arabic. The pharyngeal consonants ʿayin (ע) and ḥet (ח) are pronounced like their Arabic equivalents (ع, ح), produced deep in the throat. The qof (ק) is uvular not velar, and the tet/tav distinction is preserved. These features were standardized "out" of modern Israeli Hebrew under Ashkenazi influence in the early state — Ben-Yehuda's standardization flattened them. But Mizrahi families preserved them at home, in synagogue, and in song, and recent Israeli identity politics has reclaimed them as the more authentic register.

Cultural note — The pronunciation difference is generational and political. Older Mizrahim (born in Arab countries, ~1920-1960) speak with full pharyngeal sounds — ʿayin and ḥet are deep, the qof is uvular. Their Israeli-born children often anglicized the sounds to fit in. Now their grandchildren are reclaiming them as a marker of authentic Mizrahi identity. Mizrahi singers like Eyal Golan, Sarit Hadad, and Omer Adam pronounce with full Mizrahi character. The Maccabeats and the Mizrahi pop wave revived the sound for younger Israelis, and you'll hear it dominate Israeli radio in 2026.
