Closing Block 41 with a synthesis of Israeli cinema vocabulary and cultural anchors. From Yaakov Ben-Dov filming the British arrival in Jerusalem in 1917 to Gal Gadot opening Wonder Woman in 2017, Israeli cinema is a 110-year mirror of the country itself: pioneer mythology, Mizrahi-Ashkenazi tension, the Lebanon trauma, the occupation, the kibbutz, the religious-secular fault line, the diaspora gaze. Ten Oscar nominations, zero wins — but a cinema that punches far above its weight at Cannes, Venice, Berlin, and Sundance, and a TV industry (Hatufim, Fauda, Shtisel, Tehran) that now feeds Hollywood scripts.

Cultural note — Israeli cinema lives at the intersection of state funding (Israeli Film Fund 1979, Ophir Awards 1990), three film schools (Sam Spiegel Jerusalem, Sapir Sderot, Tisch Tel Aviv), and a director-driven auteur tradition. The "Bourekas" populist comedies of the 1960s-70s, the "New Sensibility" auteur movement led by Uri Zohar, and the post-2000s personal/political wave (Cedar, Folman, Maoz, the Elkabetz siblings) form three distinct waves. Watch Sallah Shabati, Waltz with Bashir, and Gett before any film-talk dinner in Tel Aviv — these are the canonical references everyone assumes you know.
