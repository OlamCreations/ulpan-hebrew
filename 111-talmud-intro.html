The Talmud — Structure & Approach

Lesson 111 · תַּלְמוּד
Home111

After the Tanakh, the Talmud is the second pillar of Jewish text. 2,711 double-sided pages, written in Hebrew and Aramaic across 500 years (~200-500 CE), edited in Babylon. It's not a code of law — it's a record of arguments. Every page has the Mishna (the law) at the center and the Gemara (the discussion) around it. Israelis say "talmudic" the way English-speakers say "byzantine" — for any debate that gets impossibly granular.

Cultural note — Daf Yomi (דַּף יוֹמִי, "page a day") is a global program where Jews study one Talmud page per day, finishing the whole thing in 7.5 years. The cycle started in 1923. Hundreds of thousands participate. The completion ceremony (סִיּוּם הַשַּׁ"ס) fills MetLife Stadium.

Mishna Berachot 1:1 — Opening of the Talmud

Every Daf Yomi cycle starts here. The very first Mishna asks: from when can you say the evening Shema? The text is 2000 years old. The argument hasn't ended.

Pirkei Avot 1:14 — Hillel's Three Questions

Pirkei Avot ("Ethics of the Fathers") is the most quoted tractate. Hillel's three questions are inscribed everywhere — schools, t-shirts, IDF bases.
