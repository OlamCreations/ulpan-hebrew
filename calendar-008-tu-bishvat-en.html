Tu BiShvat: New Year of the Trees

ט״ו בִּשְׁבָט · 15 SHEVAT · LATE JANUARY / EARLY FEBRUARY · ROSH HASHANA LA-ILANOT
Home Tu BiShvat
Tu BiShvat, literally "the 15th of Shevat", is the new year for trees, listed in Mishna Rosh HaShana 1:1 as one of the four new years of the Jewish calendar. Originally a technical date for agricultural tithing (which year's crop a fruit belongs to), it has become a celebration of trees and the land of Israel. In modern Israel it's like Earth Day + Arbor Day, schools plant saplings, environmental groups organize hikes, families hold a Tu BiShvat seder (originated with 16th c. Kabbalists of Safed) eating the seven species of Israel (wheat, barley, grape, fig, pomegranate, olive, date) plus other fruits, with four cups of wine (white → blush → rose → red, symbolizing the seasons).

♦ About Tu BiShvat

From a technical tax-and-tithing boundary date in the Mishnah to a celebration of trees and ecology in modern Israel. The trajectory reflects the broader Jewish reconnection to the land that political Zionism made possible.

The original meaning

Tu BiShvat marks the 'cutoff' between two fiscal-agricultural years for fruit-trees. Fruit that started to form before Tu BiShvat belongs to last year's tithe; fruit that formed after belongs to next year's. This matters because of orlah (a young tree's first 3 years of fruit may not be eaten) and the various tithes (terumah, ma'aser rishon, ma'aser sheni, ma'aser ani). For most Jews historically the day passed unnoticed.

The Safed Kabbalists

16th c., the Kabbalists of Safed (Tsfat), led by Rabbi Isaac Luria's circle, created the Pri Etz Hadar Tu BiShvat seder. Eat from the seven species + 30 other fruits, drink 4 cups of wine progressively redder, to 'repair' through eating, mystical theology of tikkun. The seder fell out of fashion until the early 20th century.

Modern revival

Zionist movement of the early 20th c. seized Tu BiShvat as a national 'tree-planting day.' The Jewish National Fund (Keren Kayemet le-Yisrael) organizes mass tree-plantings every Tu BiShvat. Schoolchildren plant saplings, for many Israelis, the iconic Tu BiShvat memory is being driven by bus to a JNF forest and planting a small pine. JNF has planted >240 million trees in Israel since 1901.

Environmental holiday

Since the 1970s Tu BiShvat has been adopted as the Jewish 'Earth Day.' Synagogues and schools hold ecology-themed sederim. Environmental activists use the day to publicize climate concerns. The fruits eaten are now often pressed for organic + fair-trade ethics. Many secular Israeli families do a casual seder, a meal with fruits + nuts + wine + a song or two.

Almond blossom

Tu BiShvat falls in early February, exactly when the almond trees bloom in Israel, the first trees to flower at the end of winter. The famous children's song Hashkediya Porachat ('The almond tree is blooming') is a Tu BiShvat staple. The blossoming almond is a sign that winter is ending and spring is coming.
