Idioms are the fingerprints of fluent speech. "Lachaval al ha-zman" literally means "what a waste of time" but actually means "wow, that was great" — positive sarcasm at its finest. "Ein matzav" means no way, impossible. "Achla" means great. "Yatza min ha-keilim" means he lost his temper, while "halach feder" means it vanished. After this lesson you can use 50+ advanced idioms, the layer that makes you sound native instead of textbook.

Cultural note — Israeli idioms blend biblical Hebrew, Yiddish, Arabic, and post-modern slang into one chaotic register. Many idioms have IRONIC meanings: "lachaval al ha-zman" is positive, and "yatza me-ha-bul" can be sarcastic praise or actual surprise depending on tone. Body-part idioms abound: "al ha-panim" (on the face) means terrible, "b\'rosh shaket" (with quiet head) means peacefully. Native Israelis pepper their speech with these constantly, and without them you sound like a tourist reading from a phrasebook.
