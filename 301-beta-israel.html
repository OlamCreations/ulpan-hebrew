Beta Israel — Ethiopian Jewish History

Lesson 301 · בֵּיתָא יִשְׂרָאֵל
Home301

Beta Israel (Ethiopian Jews, sometimes called Falasha — an older derogatory term) trace their history in Ethiopia back roughly 2000 years, claiming descent from the lost tribe of Dan or from the union of King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. They lived in northern Ethiopia (Gondar, Tigray) for centuries practicing pre-Talmudic Judaism. Population pre-aliyah: ~28,000. Today ~150,000+ Israeli citizens. Recognition by the Israeli rabbinate came in 1973 (Sephardic) and 1975 (Ashkenazic), enabling Law of Return aliyah.

Cultural note — Beta Israel practiced Judaism with key differences from Talmudic tradition: they kept biblical kashrut without Talmudic elaborations, observed Shabbat with biblical strictness, never knew Hanukkah (post-biblical), and used Ge\'ez liturgy. Their religious leaders were called kessim (priests) rather than rabbis. Persecution in Ethiopia drove waves of attempted aliyah. The community\'s experience navigates simultaneously: ancient Jewish identity, African heritage, and integration into modern Israel.
