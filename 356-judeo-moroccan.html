Haketia & Judeo-Moroccan Arabic

Lesson 356 · שָׂפוֹת יְהוּדֵי מָרוֹקוֹ
Home356

Moroccan Jewry was never linguistically monolithic. Two ancestral communities coexisted for five centuries — the Toshavim (autochthonous, pre-1492) speaking Judeo-Arabic, and the Megorashim (Spanish exiles after the expulsion) speaking Haketia, a Judeo-Spanish-Arabic creole. Add Berber-Jewish dialects in the Atlas Mountains, French from the 1912 protectorate, and Hebrew loanwords woven through everything. By the 1950s diaspora to Israel, France, Canada, this linguistic universe began collapsing. Today it is endangered. This lesson preserves the core.

Cultural note — Haketia is NOT Ladino. Both descend from Old Spanish, but Haketia (northern Morocco — Tetouan, Tangier, Larache, Asilah) absorbed heavy Moroccan Arabic and Hebrew influence, while Ladino (Salonika, Istanbul, Sarajevo) absorbed Turkish, Greek, and South Slavic. A Tetuani grandmother in Ashdod and a Salonikan grandfather in Bat Yam both speak "Judeo-Spanish" but understand each other only partially. The Toshavim of Fes, Marrakech, Meknes spoke a completely different language — Judeo-Moroccan Arabic, written in Hebrew letters, with its own regional variants.
