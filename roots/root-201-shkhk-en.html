Root שׂ/שׁ־ח־ק: playing & wearing down

Roots Atlas 201 · שֹׁרֶשׁ ש ח ק · the playing & wearing root
Home ש ח ק Morphology
ש ח ק
s/sh · ch · k
everything to do with playing a game, acting a role, and wearing something down by friction
6 words · 1 root · 3 verb-voices

Three letters spelled the same hide two senses split by a single dot. With sin (שׂ), שׂ־ח־ק is to play, a game, or a role on stage. With shin (שׁ), שׁ־ח־ק is to wear down, to erode by friction. Modern Hebrew keeps them apart by the dot but the same consonants run through both. The play branch gives us the player, the actor, and the game; the erosion branch gives us "worn out", which neatly also means "exhausted". Verified against pealim, dot included.

The verb across the binyanim (voices)

The same root runs through the verb system. Each binyan is a voice: it bends the one idea into active, passive, causative or reciprocal. Tap to hear.

Pa'al
לִשְׁחֹקlishchok
simple active, the plain action
to erode, to wear away (with shin)
Nif'al
לְהִשָּׁחֵקlehishachek
passive / middle, it happens to the subject
to be eroded, to be worn down
Pi'el
לְשַׂחֵקlesachek
intensive / specialized active
to play (a game); to play a role, to act (with sin)

The two senses sort themselves across the binyanim: pa'al and its passive nif'al carry the erosion sense (shin), while the active pi'el carries playing and acting (sin). Same skeleton, the dot tells you which world you are in.
