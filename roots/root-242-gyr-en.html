Root ג־י־ר: converting (to Judaism)

Roots Atlas 242 · שֹׁרֶשׁ ג י ר · the conversion root
Home ג י ר Morphology
ג י ר
g · y · r
everything to do with converting to Judaism and becoming part of the people
4 words · 1 root · 2 verb-voices

This root grows from one of the oldest words in Hebrew: גֵּר, the stranger who joins, the resident outsider who becomes one of the people. From it the modern verb system builds the act of conversion to Judaism: to convert someone (לְגַיֵּר) and to convert oneself, to undergo it (לְהִתְגַּיֵּר). Note the term: a גֵּר here is a convert, not merely a foreigner. The same two binyanim that recruit in ג-י-ס here bring someone into the covenant. Every word here is verified against pealim, niqqud included.

The verb across the binyanim (voices)

The same root runs through the verb system. Each binyan is a voice: it bends the one idea into active, passive, causative or reciprocal. Tap to hear.

Hitpa'el
לְהִתְגַּיֵּרlehitgayer
reflexive / reciprocal, to / on oneself
to convert to Judaism (to undergo conversion)

Like its near-twin ג-י-ס, this root lives in the pi'el / hitpa'el pair. לְגַיֵּר is done to another (a beit din converts the candidate); לְהִתְגַּיֵּר is done by oneself (you go through the conversion). No pa'al, hif'il, pu'al or huf'al are lexicalized: the act is intensive and personal, which is exactly what these two binyanim express.

Words born from the root

Same root letters, different noun moulds (mishkalim). The mould tells you the function: who does it, the act of doing it, or the thing produced.
