Kiddush Shabbat Day: Kiddusha Rabba

קִדּוּשָׁא רַבָּא · Saturday late morning before the meal · V'shamru + wine blessing
HomeKiddush Yom
Kiddusha Rabba, the Shabbat day Kiddush, said before the Saturday-morning meal (lunch) after returning from synagogue. Much shorter than the Friday-night Kiddush. The prelude is a biblical passage (Exodus 31:16-17) about Shabbat as the eternal sign between God and Israel. Then the wine blessing. No "Mekadesh haShabbat" paragraph in the morning Kiddush, the day's holiness was already sanctified Friday night. The name Kiddusha Rabba ("the Great Kiddush") is actually ironic, it's the shortest Kiddush, and the rabbis called it "the Great" euphemistically because they didn't want to call any sanctification of Shabbat "the Lesser". Said standing, ~30 seconds. Followed by HaMotzi and the lunch meal.

Read Exodus 31:16-17 (V'shamru) on Sefaria for the surrounding biblical/liturgical context.

♦ Lesson

  • When, Saturday late morning / midday, just before the Shabbat lunch meal. Said after returning from Shacharit + Musaf at synagogue.
  • How, stand. Hold the wine cup. Recite V'shamru (the biblical preamble, Ex 31:16-17). Then say the wine blessing (Borei Pri HaGafen). Drink the cup. Sit down for the meal.
  • Some communities add Zachor, before V'shamru, some recite "Zachor et yom haShabbat l'kadsho" (Ex 20:8, the Shabbat commandment from the Decalogue). This is an optional preamble; the V'shamru section is the universal core.
  • The biblical text, Exodus 31:16-17: this is the second of the two Shabbat-commandment passages in Exodus. The first (Ex 20:8-11) appears in the Ten Commandments. The second (Ex 31) reframes Shabbat as a sign of the covenant, like a wedding ring between God and Israel.
  • Vocabulary:
    • v'shamru (וְשָׁמְרוּ), "and they shall keep". Qal perfect 3rd-pl with the vav-consecutive that flips perfect to future. The word shamor ("keep!") is the Deuteronomy version of the Shabbat command ("keep" vs "remember" in Exodus).
    • l'dorotam (לְדֹרֹתָם), "throughout their generations". L' + dorot (generations) + am (their). The whole biblical phrase emphasizes generational continuity.
    • brit olam (בְּרִית עוֹלָם), "eternal covenant". Brit = covenant; same root as brit milah (the covenant of circumcision) and bar/bat mitzvah (lit. "son/daughter of the commandment").
    • ot (אוֹת), "sign". Used in the Tanakh for major covenant signs: the rainbow (Genesis 9), circumcision (Genesis 17), tefillin (Deut 6:8, "a sign on your hand"). Shabbat is one of these covenant signs.
    • vayinnafash (וַיִּנָּפַשׁ), "and He was refreshed". Niphal perfect of nun-pheh-shin, same root as nefesh (soul). Literally "and he ensouled himself", God re-souled, refreshed His being, on the seventh day. Anthropomorphic image of divine rest.

♦ About this prayer

The shorter, daytime sanctification of Shabbat. A 30-second ritual that opens the Saturday-morning meal, the second of three Shabbat meals.

Why two Kiddushim: Friday night and Saturday morning

The rabbis derive the obligation to make Kiddush twice from the dual command in the Decalogue: "zachor et yom haShabbat l'kadsho" (Ex 20:8), "Remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it." The verb l'kadsho ("to sanctify it") implies an active act of sanctification. The Sages applied this once for Friday night (the start of Shabbat) and once for Saturday day (during Shabbat itself).

However, the day Kiddush is theologically subordinate, Shabbat was already sanctified the night before, so the morning is just an additional acknowledgment. This is why it's so much shorter than the Friday-night version: there's no Mekadesh haShabbat paragraph, no full Vayechulu, just the biblical preamble plus the wine blessing.

The ironic name: "Kiddusha Rabba"

The Hebrew/Aramaic name Kiddusha Rabba means literally "the Great Kiddush". But this is the shortest of the three Shabbat Kiddushes (Friday night, Saturday morning, and the optional Mincha-time Kiddush before Seudah Shlishit). Why call the shortest "the Great"?

The Talmud (Pesachim 106a) explains: it would be inappropriate to call any of the Shabbat sanctifications "the Small" or "the Lesser". So the rabbis gave the shortest one a euphemistic name. The same kind of inverted naming is used for the central Yom Kippur prayer (called Tefilat Zakah, "the Pure Prayer") and the closing service of Yom Kippur (called Neilah, "the Closing", when in fact the gates are still open).

The wine: and the option of bread

Like the night Kiddush, the morning Kiddush is preferably said over wine in a cup of at least 86ml. If wine is unavailable, it can be said over chamar medina, any "liquor of the country" considered important in the local culture (whisky, beer, sometimes coffee in some Yemenite traditions). If even that isn't available, Kiddush is folded into HaMotzi over the bread.

In contemporary Israeli/Sephardi homes, the morning Kiddush is often a moment for grown-up children to take turns saying it, getting comfortable with the Hebrew. The shortness (~30 seconds) makes it good practice.
