Iggeret Teiman & Galut Mawza

Lesson 342 · אִגֶּרֶת תֵּימָן וְגָלוּת מַוְזַע
Home342

Two pivotal events forged Yemenite Jewish identity. Around 1172 CE, Maimonides wrote his Iggeret Teiman (Letter to Yemen) to Yaakov ben Netanel al-Fayyumi, addressed to a community crushed between Mahdist forced conversions and a false messiah claiming the end of days. Five centuries later, in 1679-1680, Imam al-Mahdi Ahmad expelled all Yemenite Jews to the Mawza coastal plain — Galut Mawza — where roughly three quarters perished from disease, heat, and famine. These two events, separated by 500 years, define how Yemenite Jews understood exile, redemption, leadership, and survival until the airlift of 1949-50.

Cultural note — Yemenite Jews call Maimonides "ha-Rambam" or "ha-Nesher ha-Gadol" (the great eagle). His Iggeret Teiman was so revered it was read publicly during persecution, copied by hand for centuries, and recited as a kind of communal psalm. Galut Mawza is remembered every year — some communities still keep a fast day, and the trauma shaped the Shami vs Baladi liturgical split that survives in Israel today.
