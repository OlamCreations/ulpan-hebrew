Persian Jewish Food Culture

Lesson 325 · אֹכֶל יְהוּדִי פַּרְסִי
Home325

Iranian Jews are one of the oldest continuous Jewish communities in the world — 2,700 years in Persia. When ~80,000 made aliyah (mostly 1948-79, then again post-Revolution), they brought a cuisine that is now one of Israel's most beloved Shabbat traditions: gondi simmering since Friday morning, tahdig fought over at the table, fesenjan's pomegranate-walnut depth, saffron threading every rice dish. Persian Jewish food is herb-forward, slow-cooked, and built around a Shabbat ritual older than Ashkenazi cholent. This lesson teaches the dishes, the techniques, and the words you'll hear in any Persian-Israeli home in Holon, Bat Yam, or Ramat Gan.

Cultural note — The Persian Jewish Shabbat table differs from Ashkenazi in a key way: rice (polo) is the centerpiece, not bread. Friday night begins with gondi soup, then khoresh stew over saffron rice with tahdig (the crispy bottom layer everyone competes for). Fresh herbs — mint, parsley, cilantro, dill, fenugreek — appear in every course. At Pesach seder, Persian Jews famously whip each other gently with green onions during Dayenu, a tradition recalling Egyptian taskmasters' whips. In Israel, Persian-Iraqi-Bukharan cuisine is grouped under "Mizrahi", but each has distinct dishes — gondi is uniquely Persian Jewish.
