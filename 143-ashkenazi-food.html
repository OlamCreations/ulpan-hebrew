Ashkenazi Cuisine

Lesson 143 · מִטְבָּח אַשְׁכְּנַזִּי
Home143

Ashkenazi food = the Eastern European Jewish kitchen — Polish, Russian, Lithuanian, Hungarian, Romanian. Heavy on root vegetables, slow-cooked meat, fish, dough. Carries Yiddish vocabulary into Hebrew. Less popular among younger Israelis but defining for Friday-night dinner culture and Jewish holidays. The classic gefilte fish, kugel, kreplach, blintzes, herring, chopped liver — every Israeli grandmother (savta) has her version.

Cultural note — Many "Israeli" foods at hotel breakfasts are actually Ashkenazi: matzah, herring, schmaltz herring, varnishkes. The classic kibbutz dining hall (חֲדַר אֹכֶל) was Ashkenazi by default for decades. Today, many Israelis under 40 jokingly call this food "סָבְתָא־פוּד" — "grandma food." But every Yom Kippur break-fast or Pesach seder, it returns.
