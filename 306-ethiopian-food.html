Ethiopian Food — Injera, Wat, Buna

Lesson 306 · אֹכֶל אֶתְיוֹפִי
Home306

Ethiopian-Israeli food culture combines biblical kashrut with Ethiopian flavor traditions. The centerpiece is injera, a fermented teff sourdough flatbread eaten by hand. Wat dishes are stews — doro wat (festival chicken stew), shiro (chickpea), gomen (greens) — and coffee ceremonies (buna) are central social rituals, three rounds, freshly roasted beans, frankincense burning. In Israel, Ethiopian restaurants concentrate in Tel Aviv (Beta Israel district), Kiryat Malachi, Petach Tikva, and Beit Shemesh, and most major cities now have at least one Ethiopian-Israeli kosher restaurant.

Cultural note — The buna (coffee ceremony) is the social heartbeat of Ethiopian-Israeli homes. Green beans are roasted on a small charcoal grill, ground fresh, brewed in a jebena (clay pot), and served in three rounds (abol, tona, baraka). Frankincense burns simultaneously. Ethiopian food is eaten communally, with hands, from one large injera platter. Vegetarian options dominate, since Ethiopian Christianity has many fasting days. For Israel, kashrut compatibility is high — most traditional Ethiopian dishes are naturally kosher.
