Modeh Ani: I Give Thanks

מוֹדֶה אֲנִי · on waking, before getting out of bed · 12 Hebrew words
HomeModeh Ani
The first prayer of the day, twelve Hebrew words said the moment consciousness returns, before the eyes even open, before getting out of bed. The text dates to Rabbi Moshe ben Makhir of Safed (16th century, Seder HaYom, c. 1599) and has been printed at the front of every siddur since. The premise: each night the soul departs to be "deposited" with God; each morning it is returned. The first conscious act is to acknowledge that gift. Modeh ani uses no divine names (YHVH, Elohim), because in traditional practice the hands have not yet been ritually washed, the body is not yet in a state of purity for invoking the full Name. So the prayer addresses God obliquely: "living and eternal King". Two minutes of memorization gives you a habit you'll keep for the rest of your life.

♦ Lesson: how to say it

  • When: the very first thing on waking, before getting out of bed, before checking your phone, before opening the eyes if possible. Said quietly to oneself; no minyan, no special posture. Many people teach it to their children as the first prayer they ever learn.
  • Gender forms: men say "modeh ani" (מוֹדֶה אֲנִי), women say "modah ani" (מוֹדָה אֲנִי), the he at the end of modah marks the feminine. Modeh/modah is a present-tense participle of יָדָה (to thank, acknowledge), used as a 1st-person present construction common in Hebrew prayer.
  • Why no divine name: the prayer addresses God as "melech chai v'kayam" ("living and eternal King") rather than using YHVH or Elohim. Reason given by the rabbis: on waking the hands have not yet been ritually washed (netilat yadayim) and the body is not yet in a state of purity for invoking the four-letter Name. So Modeh Ani is a deliberate workaround, gratitude can be offered immediately, the full Name waits.
  • The theology in one sentence: sleep is a small death; waking is a small resurrection. The Talmud (Berachot 57b) says sleep is "one sixtieth of death", and the soul is said to "ascend on high" each night for review and renewal. Modeh Ani is the acknowledgment that the soul has been returned, shehechezarta bi nishmati. Each morning is, technically, an undeserved second chance.
  • The closing biblical quote: "rabah emunatecha" ("great is Your faithfulness") is a direct quote from Lamentations 3:23, "chadashim labbekarim, rabah emunatecha" ("new every morning, great is Your faithfulness"). Jeremiah's lament over the destruction of the First Temple includes this single line of hope. Modeh Ani lifts it out and makes it a daily personal claim.
  • Pronunciation watch:
    • lefanecha (לְפָנֶיךָ), "before You". From panim (face) + cha (your, m.sg.). Standing "before God's face" = standing in His presence.
    • kayam (קַיָּם), "enduring / existing / standing". Same root as kiyum (existence) and l'kayem (to fulfill). Modern Hebrew uses kayam for "existent" in scientific contexts.
    • shehechezarta (שֶׁהֶחֱזַרְתָּ), long but regular. She- (that, relative) + he-chezar-ta (hiphil perfect 2ms of ח.ז.ר). Root ח.ז.ר means "to return / go back". Hiphil makes it causative: "You caused to return" = "You returned [something to someone]".
    • nishmati (נִשְׁמָתִי), "my soul". Neshamah + 1cs possessive suffix. In Jewish anthropology there are three names for the soul: nefesh (life-force, animal soul), ruach (spirit), and neshamah (the highest, divine breath). Modeh Ani uses neshamah, what we receive each morning is the highest tier of self.
    • chemlah (חֶמְלָה), "compassion / mercy". Closely related to rachamim (mercy, from rechem, womb). Both are about being moved to act on behalf of another.
    • emunah (אֱמוּנָה), "faithfulness / trust". Root א.מ.נ, the root of amen and omein (foster-parent, "one who is trustworthy"). Not "faith" as in belief, closer to fidelity, the reliability of a covenant partner.
  • Pace: ~6 seconds. Three short breaths. There is no melody, it's spoken or whispered. Some Sephardi traditions chant it on a low monotone; Ashkenazim usually say it quietly. Said sitting up in bed, or lying back if too tired to sit.

♦ About this prayer

Twelve Hebrew words, four hundred years old, said before the eyes open. The smallest building block of Jewish daily prayer, and a good place to start learning to pray in Hebrew.

The text: sixteenth-century Safed

The exact wording of Modeh Ani appears for the first time in Rabbi Moshe ben Makhir's Seder HaYom ("Order of the Day"), printed in Venice around 1599. Rabbi Moshe ben Makhir headed a yeshiva in Ein Zeitim, a small Galilean village near Safed, the same kabbalistic community that produced Lurianic Kabbalah, the Lecha Dodi hymn, and most of the Friday-night liturgy as we know it today.

Before 1599 there were various waking blessings, but none in this concise form. The genius of Modeh Ani is its minimalism: no divine name, no list of attributes, no formula, just gratitude, an address ("King"), and one short biblical quote ("great is Your faithfulness"). It became universally adopted within a century and is now the very first text printed at the front of every Ashkenazi and Sephardi siddur.

The grammar lesson hidden in 12 words

Modeh Ani is short, but it teaches several core Hebrew structures Jonas will encounter constantly:

1. Participle as present tense, modeh is technically a verbal adjective ("thanking"), but in prayer Hebrew it functions as "I thank". This pattern (participle + 1st person pronoun) is how Hebrew expresses "I X" in a present-tense gnomic / declarative sense. You will see baruch atah (blessed are You), melech ha'olam (King of the universe), ozer Yisrael bigevurah (girding Israel with strength), all the same participle structure.

2. Pronominal suffixes, lefanecha (before You), bi (in me), nishmati (my soul), emunatecha (Your faithfulness). Hebrew attaches possessive and prepositional pronouns directly as suffixes. Mastering these is the single biggest step toward reading prayer/Tanakh fluently. Six occurrences in 12 words.

3. The relative she-, shehechezarta means "she- (that) + hechezarta (You returned)". The single-letter prefix she- is the most common subordinating conjunction in Hebrew, used identically in modern speech (ha-ish she-amar, "the man who said").

4. Hiphil causative, hechezarta is the hiphil (causative stem) of ח.ז.ר. Qal would be chazarta ("you returned [yourself]"); hiphil hechezarta ("you caused to return / you returned [something]"). The he- prefix + internal vowel change is the hiphil signature.

The biblical layer: Lamentations 3

The closing "rabah emunatecha" ("great is Your faithfulness") quotes Lamentations 3:22-23 directly:

חַסְדֵי יְהוָה כִּי לֹא־תָמְנוּ, כִּי לֹא־כָלוּ רַחֲמָיו
חֲדָשִׁים לַבְּקָרִים, רַבָּה אֱמוּנָתֶךָ

"The kindnesses of the LORD have not ceased, His mercies have not ended, they are new every morning; great is Your faithfulness."

Lamentations was written after the destruction of the First Temple (586 BCE), a sequence of five poems mourning Jerusalem in ruins. Chapter 3 is the structural and emotional center: a personal lament that suddenly, in the middle, breaks into one paragraph of hope (verses 22-24) before returning to grief. Modeh Ani extracts the very phrase from that paragraph and plants it at the start of every waking day. The hidden message: even in personal or national exile, every morning is a fresh covenant.

Three theological moves in twelve words

1. Death and resurrection, daily. The premise is that sleep is a small death, the soul departs nightly to be reviewed, weighed, refreshed. Waking is therefore a daily miniature resurrection. This isn't metaphor in the kabbalistic reading, the soul literally rises (Talmud, Berachot 57b; Zohar). Modeh Ani is gratitude for an event we sleep through every night without noticing.

2. Gratitude before identity. Hebrew word order is the message. The prayer doesn't start "Ani modeh" (I thank), it starts "Modeh ani" (Thanking, I am). The thanks comes before the self. Rabbi Schneur Zalman of Liadi (the Tanya, 18th c.) reads this as a deliberate inversion: in the new morning, the act of thanking is what re-establishes the "I". You don't thank because you exist; you exist because you thank.

3. Faithfulness is the divine attribute being celebrated. Not chesed (kindness), not rachamim (mercy), not gevurah (might), emunah: reliability. The pattern of God's behavior is what's praised. Every morning, again, the soul comes back. The covenant holds.

How to integrate it into your daily life

1. Memorize the twelve words. Total time: under five minutes. Print the page or screenshot the verses.

2. For the first week, say it in Hebrew on waking (whispered, eyes still closed). Then say the English meaning to yourself silently, slowly, once.

3. By week two, drop the English. The Hebrew has internalized.

4. After Modeh Ani comes washing of the hands (netilat yadayim), a small ritual that can be done at any sink with any cup. Then Birkot HaShachar (the morning blessings) or the rest of Shacharit if you're davening fully. Modeh Ani alone is enough as a daily anchor while you build the rest of your practice.
