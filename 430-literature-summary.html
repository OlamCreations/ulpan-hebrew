Block 43 Summary — Hebrew Literature

Closing Block 43 with a synthesis of Hebrew literature from Haskalah (18c) to today. Across nine lessons we covered the founders (Mendelssohn, Mapu, Mendele, Ben-Yehuda), the national poets (Bialik, Tchernichovsky), the Nobel laureate (Agnon), modern poetry (Amichai, Alterman, Goldberg, Ravikovitch, Wallach, Mizrahi voices), the big three novelists (Yehoshua, Oz, Grossman), contemporary fiction (Keret, Kashua, Nevo), children's literature, and the prizes and publishing houses that structure the field. This lesson consolidates the 50 essential names, dates, works, and institutions.

Cultural note — Hebrew literature is unique in world history: a language reborn from sacred-only use in the 19c, with one Nobel Prize (Agnon 1966), one of the most translated living poets ever (Amichai), and a contemporary scene that produces Booker International winners (Grossman 2017). The map runs Berlin → Odessa → Tel Aviv → Jerusalem. Knowing twenty names and ten dates lets you walk into any Israeli bookstore, salon conversation, or literary section of Haaretz without bluffing.
