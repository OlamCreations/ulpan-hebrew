Judeo-Tunisian & Judeo-Algerian Arabic

Two sister Judeo-Arabic varieties of the Maghreb. Judeo-Tunisian — qeltu-style, distinct from the Muslim Tunisian gilit dialect, written in Hebrew script for centuries, layered with Italian (Livornese) from the Grana merchant community of Tunis and French from the 1881 protectorate. Judeo-Algerian — three regional varieties (Constantine, Algiers, Oran), reshaped by the 1870 Crémieux decree that made Algerian Jews French citizens overnight, accelerating French overlay and language shift. A small Berber-Jewish enclave persisted in the Mzab valley until the mid-20th century. Today maybe a few thousand speakers worldwide, mostly in Israel and France. The National Library of Israel has been digitizing recordings and prayer books to preserve what remains.

Cultural note — The Crémieux Decree (October 24, 1870) granted French citizenship to ~37,000 Algerian Jews — but excluded Muslims. This single act split the Algerian linguistic landscape: Jewish communities pivoted toward French within a generation, while Muslim communities stayed Arabic-dominant. By 1962 (independence), most Algerian Jews left for France or Israel — taking their qeltu Arabic with them. Tunisia followed a different path: the 1881 protectorate and 1956 independence preserved Judeo-Tunisian longer because Italian and French overlay was layered on top of Arabic, not replacing it. The Grana (Livornese-origin Jews of Tunis) spoke a particular Judeo-Tunisian flavored with Italian — a third stratum the Tunis-born Touansa community rarely shared. In Israeli families today, you can hear grandparents code-switching across four languages mid-sentence.
