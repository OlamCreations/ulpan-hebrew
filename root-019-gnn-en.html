Root ג־נ־ן — garden & shield

ג נ נ
g · n · n
everything to do with enclosing, protecting, the garden
8 words · 1 root · 3 verb-voices

One image sits under this whole family: a fence around something precious. A garden (גַּן) is ground you wall off and tend; a shield (מָגֵן) is the wall you hold up in battle; to defend is to throw that wall around a person. The same three letters give you both the gardener who cultivates and the protector who guards — because enclosing to grow and enclosing to shelter are the same gesture. This is a geminate root (the נ doubles), so the protective idea is literally built into its repeating sound. Every word here is verified against pealim, niqqud included.

The verb across the binyanim (voices)

The same root runs through the verb system. Each binyan is a voice: it bends the one idea into active, passive, causative or reciprocal. Tap to hear.

Pa'al
לָגֹןlagon
simple active — the plain action
to defend, to shield (biblical)
Pi'el
לְגַנֵּןleganen
intensive / specialized active
to garden, to do gardening (rare)
Hif'il
לְהָגֵןlehagen
causative — make someone do it
to defend, to protect

The two protective verbs split by register: the pa'al לָגֹן is biblical and archaic, while modern Hebrew defends with the hif'il לְהָגֵן. The pi'el לְגַנֵּן carries the other face of the root — tending a garden — though it stays rare. The passive and reflexive shells are not lexicalized for ג־נ־ן.
