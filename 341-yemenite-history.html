Yemenite Jewry — Ancient Origins

Lesson 341 · יְהוּדֵי תֵּימָן הַקְּדוּמִים
Home341

The Yemenite Jewish community is one of the oldest continuous Jewish communities in the world, with traditions claiming roots stretching 2500+ years to the era before the destruction of the First Temple. This lesson covers the historical layers — pre-Temple settlement legends, the Queen of Sheba narrative, the post-586 BCE Babylonian exile dispersal, the extraordinary Himyar Kingdom (4th-6th c. CE) when a Jewish king ruled southern Arabia, the Aksumite wars that ended that kingdom, and the distinct linguistic and religious patterns that preserved an archaic Hebrew tradition isolated from European Jewry for two millennia.

Cultural note — Yemenite Hebrew preserves features many scholars argue are closer to Biblical pronunciation than any other surviving tradition: the distinction between gimel/ghimel, dalet/dhalet, and a vav pronounced as "w". When the State of Israel debated standard Hebrew pronunciation in the 1950s, several linguists argued for the Yemenite pattern as the most authentic. The Sephardic-Israeli compromise won, but every scholar of Biblical Hebrew still studies Yemenite recitation as the closest living window into how Hebrew sounded before the diaspora.
