Modern Hebrew literature is one of the world\'s youngest literary traditions yet has produced Nobel laureates, world classics, and a unique voice. Shai Agnon won the Nobel Prize in 1966. Amos Oz, A.B. Yehoshua, and David Grossman became globally translated voices in the 80s-2000s. Etgar Keret mastered the post-modern Israeli short story. Reading literary Hebrew (slightly more formal than journalism) opens an entire layer of cultural depth.

Cultural note — Hebrew literature reflects the national psyche: anxieties about identity, war, peace, family, the Holocaust, the diaspora. Oz wrote about the kibbutz; Yehoshua about Sephardic identity; Grossman about the West Bank and grief. Etgar Keret\'s stories are short, surreal, often funny, and enormously popular abroad. Bookstores still thrive in Israel; serious reading remains cultural currency.
