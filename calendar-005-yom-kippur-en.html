Yom Kippur: Day of Atonement

יוֹם כִּפּוּר · 10 TISHREI · 25-HOUR FAST · 5 SERVICES · KOL NIDREI → NEILAH
Home Yom Kippur
Yom Kippur is the holiest day of the Jewish year, even most secular Israelis observe it. A 25-hour total fast (no food, no water) from sunset on 9 Tishrei to nightfall on 10 Tishrei. Five prohibitions: no eating/drinking, no washing, no anointing, no wearing leather shoes, no marital relations. The day is spent in synagogue with five services: Maariv (Kol Nidrei), Shacharit, Musaf, Mincha, and the unique closing Neilah ("locking of the gates"). The shofar is blown one final tekiah at the very end. In Israel: no broadcast TV/radio, no flights, no cars on the roads (children bike in empty streets, the iconic image). White is worn (purity, shrouds, the angels). The book of Jonah is read at Mincha.

The Mishnah's famous teaching that defines the limit of Yom Kippur. Sins against God, atoned. Sins against people, only after you've personally asked forgiveness from them. This is why the days before YK are spent making phone calls, sending messages, asking forgiveness from anyone you might have wronged.

♦ About Yom Kippur

The most observed Jewish holiday in Israel, even avowed secularists fast, even cars stop. The streets of Tel Aviv on Yom Kippur are an icon: completely empty of traffic, children riding bikes on freeways, almost total quiet across the country. Synagogues full; many secular Israelis come for Kol Nidrei.

Kol Nidrei

The opening service, sung before sunset on the eve. The text is in Aramaic (not Hebrew) and annuls all vows made between this Yom Kippur and next, a legal-religious clean slate. The melody is among the most haunting in the Jewish musical tradition (used by Bruch in his cello piece, by Beethoven indirectly in Op. 131). Even arriving late to Yom Kippur services is considered a major loss because Kol Nidrei sets the entire emotional register.

Neilah: the closing

Unique to Yom Kippur. As the sun sets at the end of the fast, the doors of the ark are opened and remain open. The image: the gates of heaven are slowly closing, last chance to slip in our prayers. Built up over ~60 minutes to a climax: the Shema Yisrael shouted in unison, Baruch shem kevod malchuto le'olam va'ed (three times), Hashem hu ha-Elohim ('the LORD is God', seven times, echoing Elijah at Mt Carmel, 1 Kings 18:39). Then one long shofar blast, the fast ends.

The Avodah service

During Musaf, the entire Temple-era Yom Kippur ritual is re-enacted in poetic form: the High Priest's confessions, the lottery of the two goats (one to God, one to Azazel), the entering of the Holy of Holies. When the High Priest is described falling on his face and saying the Tetragrammaton, the congregation falls to the ground (kor'im), one of the few times Jews prostrate fully in prayer.

The fast

25 hours, no food, no water. Begins ~18 minutes before sunset on 9 Tishrei, ends at nightfall on 10 Tishrei (3 stars visible). Children under bar/bat mitzvah age don't fast; pregnant/nursing women generally don't (consult a rabbi); the seriously ill are forbidden to fast. Most secular Israelis fast even if they don't go to synagogue, it's the national rhythm, not just religious obligation.

Breaking the fast

After the final tekiah, families return home for a 'break-fast' meal. Typical foods: light, easily digestible, soup, fish, dairy. In Sephardic homes: orange juice + sweet pastry. In Ashkenazic: herring + kichel. Then immediately: building the sukkah for Sukkot, which begins in 4 days. The cycle moves forward.
