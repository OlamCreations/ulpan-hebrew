Tunisian Aliyah & Exodus

Lesson 363 · עֲלִיַּת יְהוּדֵי תּוּנִיסְיָה
Home363

The Tunisian Jewish community traces back over 2,000 years, with the Djerba synagogue (El Ghriba) among the oldest in the world. Roughly 100,000 Jews lived in Tunisia at independence in 1948. Within 25 years, that number collapsed to under 10,000. Half went to France, half to Israel — a split that defines the diaspora today. This lesson follows the demographic, political, and violent inflections that emptied a 2-millennia-old community.

Cultural note — Tunisian Jews who chose Israel mostly settled in Be'er Sheva, Lod, Petah Tikva, Dimona, and Ashkelon. They brought a distinct Maghrebi liturgy, a Judeo-Arabic dialect, and culinary traditions (couscous au poisson, brik, kemia). The Djerba pilgrimage at Lag BaOmer still draws thousands annually — including from Israel — despite the 2002 and 2023 attacks. The Tunisian-Israeli identity is one of the strongest in the Mizrahi community: more cohesive than Moroccan, more politically active than Algerian.
