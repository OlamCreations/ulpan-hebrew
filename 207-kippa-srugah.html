Kippa Srugah — The Knitted Kippa

Lesson 207 · כִּיפָּה סְרוּגָה
Home207

The kippa srugah (knitted kippa) is the visible identity marker of religious-Zionist (dati le'umi) Jewish men in Israel — worn by ~10-15% of Jewish men. It distinguishes its wearer from the kippa shchora (black velvet, haredi), the kippa lavan (white, Yom Kippur), and the kippa Bukharit (large embroidered, Sephardic ethnic). Often handmade by a mother, girlfriend, or wife, sometimes carrying initials or designs, the kippa srugah carries the weight of a personal bond. In Israeli social shorthand, "kippa srugah" instantly tags political-religious identity.

Cultural note — Reading kippot is an Israeli reflex. Big knitted kippa = mainstream dati le'umi. Small knitted kippa = "datlash" (formerly religious) or modernist. Black velvet kippa = haredi/charedi. White kippa = special holidays. No kippa = chiloni or masorti. Beyond the kippa, the chultzat dati (white shirt for Shabbat), tzitzit visible or tucked, and the way someone says "shalom" all transmit the same signal in seconds. The kippa is the loudest signal but never the only one.
