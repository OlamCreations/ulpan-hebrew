Mimouna Festival

Lesson 354 · מִימוּנָה
Home354

Mimouna is the celebration that opens the night following the last day of Pesach. Born in the Jewish communities of Morocco, it is the moment when chametz (leavened food) becomes permitted again — and the moment Jewish neighbors traditionally welcomed Muslim friends into their homes for sweet table, music, and blessings. In Israel since 1966 it has become a national festival drawing political leaders and crowds of thousands, while preserving its quiet domestic core: open door, mufleta hot off the pan, and the greeting "Tarbiha umbarka" — may you be blessed and successful.

Cultural note — The open door is the heart of Mimouna. In Moroccan Jewish quarters, families left their doors literally unlocked through the night. Neighbors of all faiths walked from house to house, tasting mufleta, drinking sweet mint tea, blessing the host. The Israeli politicians who attend public Mimounas every year (every PM since Begin) are continuing — performatively or sincerely — that same gesture: the festival of crossing a threshold someone else opened for you.
