After Oz, Yehoshua, and Grossman built the canon, a new generation took over. Born 1960s-1980s, writing through Oslo, the Second Intifada, the start-up nation boom, the social-protest summer of 2011, and the pandemic. Their Hebrew is sharper, more colloquial, more fragmented. They write micro-fiction (Keret), Arab-Israeli identity (Kashua), Mizrahi memory (Matalon, Rabinyan), apartment-block intimacy (Nevo), and historical reconstruction (Iczkovits). The Sapir Prize since 2000 has crowned most of them. This lesson maps the names you will hear in Tel Aviv bookshops and at Jerusalem literary festivals.

Cultural note — The Sapir Prize (Pras Sapir, since 2000, sponsored by Mifal HaPais lottery) is the Israeli Booker — 150,000 NIS to the year׳s best Hebrew novel. Winners since 2010 include Iczkovits, Nevo (multiple shortlists), Baram, and Shilo. Ask any Israeli reader under 50 about contemporary fiction and they will name Keret first. He is the generational voice — short, dark, funny, untranslatable but translated into 45+ languages.
