The Druze Community in Israel

Lesson 183 · דְּרוּזִים
Home183

About 150,000 Druze live in Israel — a unique religious-cultural community concentrated on Mt Carmel and across the Galilee. Their religion is monotheistic, esoteric, and originated in 11th-century Egypt; it has been closed to converts since 1043 CE. Druze men have served in the IDF since 1956 under a dedicated conscription law, an arrangement unique among Arab citizens of Israel, and most identify strongly as Israeli. The major villages are Daliyat al-Karmel, Isfiya, Beit Jann, Hurfeish, and Yarka. Druze are distinct from both Israeli Arabs and Jews, with their own civic identity and a long-standing covenant of loyalty to the state in which they live.

Cultural note — The Druze faith rests on five key prophets, secret scriptures read only by the religious initiated, and a strong communal ethic. The community is split into two tiers: the uqal (initiated, who read the Hikma texts and wear the laffa) and the jahil (uninitiated, the majority, who follow ethical conduct without textual access). Loyalty to the state in which they live is a religious principle rooted in the taqiyya tradition, which is why Druze service in the IDF is not just civic but theological. Famous Druze include Knesset members, IDF generals, and chefs — and Druze villages are popular weekend destinations for knafeh, druze pita on the saj, and legendary hospitality.
