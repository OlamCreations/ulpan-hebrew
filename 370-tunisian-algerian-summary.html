Block 37 Summary — Tunisian-Algerian Jewry

Lesson 370 · סִכּוּם בְּלוֹק 37 · יַהֲדוּת תּוּנִיסְיָה וְאַלְגֶ\'ירְיָה
Home370

Block 37 closed the Maghreb arc with the two communities that lived under the longest French shadow: Tunisia, where the protectorate (1881-1956) sat on top of millennia-old Jewish settlement, and Algeria, where the Crémieux Decree of October 24, 1870 collectively naturalized roughly 37,000 Jews into French citizenship overnight. Lesson 370 is the synthesis: from Carthaginian-era origins on Djerba, through Almohad collapse, the 1492 Sephardi reshuffling that produced the Twansa/Grana split, the Crémieux trauma, Vichy abrogation 1940-43, the Bizerte crisis 1961, the Six-Day War 1967, and the 1962 Algerian Independence exodus that emptied Algeria of its Jews in a matter of months. You finish this block knowing the food (brik, fricassé, pkaila, harissa, mahia), the music (Mâlûf, Reinette l\'Oranaise, Lili Boniche, Maurice El Medioni, Enrico Macias), the language (Judeo-Tunisian, Judeo-Algerian, French as identity passport), and the writers (Albert Memmi, Jacques Derrida, Hélène Cixous) who carried this world into French intellectual life. The pied-noir juif diaspora — roughly 300,000 strong in France today — is the silent majority of French Jewry.

Cultural anchor — Tunisian and Algerian Jewry share the Maghreb but diverge in trajectory. Tunisia kept a moderate path under Bourguiba: Jewish institutions survived, aliyah was steady (~50,000 between 1948-71), the Djerba community persists today (~1,000 souls). Algeria detonated in 1962: the Crémieux Decree had so deeply Frenchified Algerian Jewry that Independence read as expulsion of "Frenchmen" — ~140,000 Jews left in months, almost all to France, almost none to Israel. This is why "pied-noir juif" is overwhelmingly Algerian, why the Israeli-Tunisian community is large but the Israeli-Algerian community is small, and why French Jewish identity today is so heavily Maghrebi-Algerian.
