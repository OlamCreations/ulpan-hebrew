Tunisian-Algerian Jewry — Origins

Lesson 361 · יְהוּדֵי תּוּנִיסְיָה וְאַלְגִ'ירְיָה — שׁוֹרָשִׁים
Home361

More than two thousand years of Jewish presence in what is now Tunisia and Algeria — older than Rome's grip on North Africa, older than Islam, older than most European Jewish communities. The story begins with Phoenician-Carthaginian traders, deepens with the post-70 CE diaspora, takes a Berber-Jewish turn under the legendary queen Kahena, survives the Arab conquest, absorbs Sephardi refugees in 1492, and produces a unique split in Tunis between indigenous Twansa and Italian-origin Grana. Lesson 361 lays the foundations.

Cultural note — North African Jewry is not Sephardi by default. The label "Sephardi" applies properly to descendants of 1492 Spanish exiles. The older indigenous communities — Berber-speaking, Carthaginian-rooted, Roman-era — are more accurately called "Maghrebi" or "Toshavim" (residents). The 1492 wave layered onto, but did not erase, this ancient substratum. In Tunis the split became formal: Twansa (indigenous) versus Grana (Livorno Italians) maintained separate synagogues, courts, and cemeteries until the 20th century.
