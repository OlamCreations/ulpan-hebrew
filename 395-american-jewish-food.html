American Jewish Food Culture

Lesson 395 · אֳכֶל יְהוּדִי-אָמֶרִיקָאִי
Home395

American Jewish cuisine is its own thing. It started Ashkenazi (Eastern European Yiddish-speaking immigrants pouring into the Lower East Side from the 1880s) and got Americanized over four generations into something neither Polish nor Israeli — a distinct culinary tradition built around the bagel, the deli, Shabbat dinner, and a few stubborn holiday foods that refuse to disappear. This lesson walks the foods, the institutions (Katz׳s, Russ & Daughters, Zabar׳s), and the Hebrew/Yiddish vocabulary to talk about all of it. Bonus: why American Jews eat Chinese food on Christmas.

Cultural note — Most "Jewish food" Americans know is Ashkenazi (from German/Polish/Russian/Lithuanian Jews) — bagels, lox, pastrami, latkes, kugel. Sephardic and Mizrahi cuisines (bourekas, sabich, jachnun, shakshuka) were rare in the US until Israeli immigration post-1970s changed the landscape. Today an Israeli-American fusion is emerging — sabich shops in Brooklyn, shakshuka brunches in LA. The "deli renaissance" (Wise Sons in SF, Kenny & Zuke׳s in Portland, Mile End in Brooklyn) is bringing back hand-cured pastrami after decades of decline. Christmas Chinese food is real — origin: 1900s NYC, only restaurants open on Dec 25 in Jewish/Chinese immigrant neighborhoods were Chinese, and the food was treif-adjacent but not pork-or-dairy-mixed in obvious ways. The tradition stuck.
