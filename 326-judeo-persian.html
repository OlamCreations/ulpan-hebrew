Home326

Judeo-Persian: Persian written in Hebrew letters, spoken across a 2,500-year corridor from Isfahan to Bukhara to LA Pico-Robertson. A literature older than modern Persian itself in some senses, regional dialects (Bukhori, Judeo-Tati, Jidi), and a code-switching tradition that today plays out three-way Persian-Hebrew-English among Iranian-Israelis and Persian-American Jews. This lesson covers the literary, dialectal, lexical, and sociolinguistic layers.

Cultural note — The word "pardes" (orchard, paradise) entered Hebrew from Old Persian "paridaida" (walled garden) — same root that gave English "paradise" via Greek "paradeisos". Persian-Hebrew contact is not a footnote: it predates Mishnaic Hebrew. When you hear an Iranian-Israeli switch from Farsi to Hebrew mid-sentence in Givatayim or Ramat-Gan, you are watching a 2,500-year linguistic relationship that survived the Achaemenids, the Sassanids, the Safavids, the Pahlavis, and the 1979 revolution.
