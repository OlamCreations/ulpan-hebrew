The second active pair. Pi'el historically intensifies or makes a root transitive, but in modern Hebrew it is just as often the binyan a verb simply lives in, especially borrowed and invented verbs (טִלְפֵּן "phoned", מִחְזֵר "recycled"). Pu'al is its passive twin, which today shows up mostly as a "ready-made" adjective (מְפֻרְסָם famous, מְסֻדָּר arranged). The shapes are sharp and easy to spot.

Pi'el, three signposts, A dagesh doubles the middle root letter. Past mould _ִ_ֵּ_ (diber, limed, bikesh). Present mould מְ_ַ_ֵּ_ with a leading מ (medaber, mevakesh, melamed). The verbal noun is the very productive _ִי_וּ_ (dibur, tiyul, bikur), covered fully in M6.
Pu'al, the u that means "it was done", Pure passive of pi'el, marked by a u vowel: past _ֻ_ַּ_ (dubar, bukash), present מְ_ֻ_ָּ_ (medubar, mevukash). You will meet it most as an adjective: מְפֻרְסָם (famous = "made-famous"), מְתֻרְגָּם (translated), מְבֻקָּשׁ (in demand). See a מ + u shape → "something was done to it".

One root, two binyanim

From "X did the action" (pi'el) to "the action was done to it" (pu'al). The u vowel is the flip.
