Sukkot: The Feast of Booths

סֻכּוֹת · 15-22 TISHREI · 7+1 DAYS · 4 SPECIES · BUILD A SUKKAH · ZMAN SIMCHATENU
Home Sukkot
Sukkot is the third of the Three Pilgrimage Festivals (Shalosh Regalim, Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot). Eight days total (seven in Israel + Shemini Atzeret/Simchat Torah). The two central commandments: dwell in a sukkah (temporary booth) for 7 days, and take the four species, lulav (palm branch), etrog (citron), hadas (myrtle), arava (willow), bound and waved. Sukkot recalls the 40-year wandering in the wilderness (the booths the Israelites lived in) and the autumn harvest. The 8th day is Shemini Atzeret / Simchat Torah, when the Torah-reading cycle restarts. Sukkot is also called Zman Simchatenu (the season of our joy).

The Torah commands actually living in a sukkah for seven days. Two interpretations of basukkot: 1) the literal physical booths the Israelites built in the wilderness, or 2) the clouds of glory (Rashi) that surrounded them. Either way: the message is the same, radical impermanence, total dependence on God, joy in fragility.

The biblical formulation. The rabbis identified the four species precisely: etrog = citron (fragrant + flavored), lulav = palm (flavored, no fragrance), hadas = myrtle (fragrant, no taste), arava = willow (neither). Midrash: the four = four types of Jews; all bound together. The species are waved in six directions (4 + up + down), God's presence is everywhere.

♦ About Sukkot

The most physical of all Jewish holidays. You actually build something. You eat under stars. You wave plants. Israel during Sukkot: balconies and gardens explode with temporary structures, the smell of fresh schach (palm fronds), specialty stores selling etrogim selected like jewels under magnifying glasses.

Building the sukkah

Halachic requirements: minimum 7 hand-breadths × 7 hand-breadths (about 70cm × 70cm). At least 2 full walls + 1 partial wall. The roof (schach) must be of organic material that grew from the ground but is no longer attached, palm fronds, bamboo mats, branches. The schach must provide more shade than sun, but you must be able to see the stars through it at night. In Israel: prefab kits sold at every hardware store starting Yom Kippur. Some families' sukkahs are elaborate: lights, decorations, hanging fruits, kids' artwork.

Eating + sleeping in the sukkah

Required: eat all bread-meals in the sukkah for 7 days. Tradition: sleep there too (weather permitting, Israel's October weather usually cooperates). On the first night, there's a specific obligation to eat at least an olive's volume of bread in the sukkah; the rest is optional but observed by religious Jews. If it rains so heavily the food gets ruined: you're exempt, God is 'sending you out of His house.'

Waving the four species

Done each morning of Sukkot (except Shabbat) during the Hallel prayer. The lulav-bundle is held in the right hand, etrog in the left, joined together. Waved in six directions: east, south, west, north, up, down. Done especially at four points in Hallel. Also: during the Hoshanot processions around the bima (one circuit each day, seven on Hoshana Rabba).

Hoshana Rabba

The 7th day. Considered the 'final sealing' of the High Holiday judgment that began on Rosh HaShana. 7 hakafot around the bima with the four species. Then: a separate bundle of 5 willow branches is beaten on the ground 5 times, the leaves fall off, an ancient ritual whose meaning is debated (related to rain, atonement, or 'striking down evil'). Many stay awake the night before studying Mishna and Tanach.

Shemini Atzeret + Simchat Torah

The 8th day, technically a separate holiday but practically continuous. In Israel: one day, combined with Simchat Torah. In diaspora: two days, Simchat Torah is the 9th day. Tefillat HaGeshem (prayer for rain) begins, Israel's rainy season starts. On Simchat Torah: the final parasha (V'zot HaBeracha, Deut 33-34) is read, then immediately the first parasha (Bereshit, Gen 1), the cycle never breaks. Dancing in synagogue with the scrolls until late at night. Outside: in Jerusalem and many cities, public hakafot in the streets.
