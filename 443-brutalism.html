Israeli Brutalism

Lesson 443 · בְּרוּטָלִיזְם יִשְׂרְאֵלִי
Home443

Israeli Brutalism — the rough-concrete civic architecture of the 1960s-70s — is the country\'s second great architectural moment after Bauhaus TLV. The Knesset (1966), the Hebrew University Givat Ram campus, the Tel Aviv Cinematheque, the Negev Brigade Monument, the IDF general staff campus (HaKirya): all share the heavy, unornamented, exposed-concrete language championed by Ram Karmi, Avraham Yaski, Zvi Hecker, and Alfred Mansfeld. This lesson covers the canonical works, the architects, and the contemporary critical revaluation.

Cultural note — For decades Israeli brutalism was treated as ugly socialist concrete to be insulated, painted, or demolished. Since the 2010s a reverse current — driven by Instagram-era aesthetics and serious scholarship at the Tel Aviv Museum — has rehabilitated it as a defining national style. The buildings remain controversial: monumental, often hot, sometimes alienating, but unmistakably Israeli.
