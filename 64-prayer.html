Core Hebrew prayers every Israeli knows by heart — including secular ones who absorbed them through school, weddings, and funerals. Here we go beyond vocabulary: the actual texts, line by line, with transliteration and translation. The Shema, key Amida blessings, the meal blessings, Kaddish.

Cultural note — Even chiloni (secular) Israelis recognize the opening lines of these prayers. Knowing the Shema is like knowing your national anthem — it's identity, not theology. The texts here are 2000-3000 years old and unchanged.

The Shema — Full Text

The central declaration of Jewish faith. Recited morning and evening, and at the moment of death. Deuteronomy 6:4-9.

V'Ahavta (You Shall Love) — Continuation

The first paragraph following the Shema. The commandment to love God with all one's heart, soul, and might.

Amida — Opening Blessing (Avot)

The first of 18 (now 19) benedictions in the silent standing prayer. Recited 3× daily by religious Jews. Called "Avot" — Patriarchs.

Mourner's Kaddish

Said by mourners for 11 months after a parent's death and on yahrtzeit (anniversary). In Aramaic, not Hebrew. No mention of death — only sanctification of God's name.

Birkat HaMazon — Opening (Grace After Meals)

Said after any meal containing bread. The opening blessing — "He who feeds the entire world with His goodness." Full version has 4 blessings.

Modeh Ani — Morning Wake-Up

First words said upon waking. Children learn this at age 3. 12 words long.

Aleinu — Closing Prayer

Closes every prayer service. Author: Rav (3rd century Babylonia).
