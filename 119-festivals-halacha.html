Lesson 119 · הִלְכוֹת חַגִּים
Home119

Lesson 19 introduced the holidays as cultural events. This one covers the actual halacha: which days are biblical (Torah-mandated) vs rabbinic, what work is forbidden, what unique mitzvot belong to each. Israeli law mirrors halacha — public buses stop on Yom Kippur, kosher-for-Passover signs cover supermarkets in March-April, sukkot pop up everywhere in October.

Cultural note — In Israel, biblical festivals are 1 day; in the diaspora, 2 days (because in ancient times the new month was uncertain by the time messengers reached far places). The phrase "יוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי שֶׁל גָּלֻיּוֹת" — "second day of festivals of exiles" — is still used today. Olim from abroad sometimes keep both days for a few years; eventually most adopt the Israeli single day.

Vayikra 23:1-4 — The Festival Calendar

The opening of the festival list. The phrase "מִקְרָאֵי קֹדֶשׁ" (sacred convocations) is the legal term for biblical festivals.

Avinu Malkeinu — High Holy Days Plea (excerpt)

Said throughout the 10 days of repentance and on Yom Kippur. The closing line is sung loudly by the entire congregation — one of the emotional peaks of the year.
