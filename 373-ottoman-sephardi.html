Ottoman Welcome & Sephardi Diaspora

Lesson 373 · קַבָּלַת הָעוֹתְ׳מָאנִים וְהַתְּפוּצָה הַסְּפָרַדִּית
Home373

In 1492, as Spain expelled its Jews, an unlikely savior opened his gates. Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire reportedly mocked Ferdinand: "they call him a wise king, this one who impoverishes his country and enriches mine." Tens of thousands of Sephardim crossed the Mediterranean to Istanbul, Salonika, Edirne, Izmir, Bursa, and Tzfat. Salonika became the only major European city with a Jewish majority — Madre de Israel. Tzfat became the cradle of Lurianic Kabbalah. Italy, Amsterdam, and the New World absorbed those who could not reach Ottoman lands. This lesson traces that fork in Jewish history.

Cultural note — The Ottoman welcome of 1492 was not pure altruism. Bayezid II understood economics: Sephardim brought capital, languages (often six or seven), printing presses, medical training, diplomatic networks, and Mediterranean trade contacts. Within a generation, Salonika produced the empire\'s textile uniforms; Istanbul Jews dominated court medicine and finance. The Ladino-speaking Sephardi world that emerged stretched from Sarajevo to Cairo to Aleppo and lasted 450 years — until the Holocaust erased Salonika in 1943. Today only fragments remain: a few synagogues in Istanbul and Izmir, the Tzfat old city, and the Ladino songs your grandparents may still half-remember.
