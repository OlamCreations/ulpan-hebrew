1979 Revolution & Jewish Exodus

Lesson 322 · הַמַּהְפֵּכָה הָאִירָאנִית וִיצִיאַת הַיְּהוּדִים
Home322

February 1, 1979: Khomeini lands in Tehran. Within months, the Pahlavi era — under which Iran's 100,000 Jews had lived in relative prosperity — collapses into a theocratic republic. By May, Habib Elghanian, the patriarch of Tehran's Jewish community, is hanged for "Zionist espionage." The signal is unmistakable. Over the next four decades, more than 90% of Iran's Jews leave — to Israel via Operation Cyrus, to Los Angeles forming "Tehrangeles," to Europe. This lesson covers the vocabulary of revolution, persecution, escape, and resettlement.

Cultural note — Iranian Jews are one of the oldest continuous Jewish communities on earth (2,700+ years, since the Babylonian exile). They do not see themselves as Mizrahi or Sephardi — they are Persian Jews, with their own liturgy, Judeo-Persian dialect, and distinct cuisine. In Israel, they cluster in Bat Yam, Holon, Or Yehuda, and Netanya. In LA, "Tehrangeles" (Westwood/Beverly Hills) is the largest Iranian Jewish diaspora city in the world — roughly 60,000 strong, with synagogues like Nessah, Eretz-SIAMAK, and Sinai Temple. When you meet a Persian Jew in Israel, ask about their family's escape route — almost every family has a Pakistan-or-Turkey story.

The Elghanian Execution — A Turning Point

Habib Elghanian (1912-1979), industrialist and head of Tehran's Jewish community, was arrested on May 8 and hanged the next day on charges of "Zionist espionage" and "corruption on earth." His execution sent a clear message to Iran's 100,000 Jews — and triggered the first wave of mass departure.

A Family's Escape — Tehran to Tel Aviv via Pakistan

A composite dialogue based on testimonies of Iranian Jewish families who escaped during the 1980s — paying smugglers in Zahedan, crossing the Baluchistan desert, reaching Karachi, then Vienna via HIAS, then Israel.
