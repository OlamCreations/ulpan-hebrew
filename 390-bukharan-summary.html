Block 39 Summary: Bukharan, Caucasian, and Georgian Jewry

Lesson 390 · סִכּוּם בְּלוֹק 39 — יַהֲדוּת בּוּכָרָה, קַוְקָז וּגְרוּזְיָה

Block 39 traced three communities that lived for two millennia at the eastern edge of the Jewish world, beyond the Persian and Ottoman frontiers: the Bukharan Jews of Central Asia (Uzbekistan, Tajikistan), the Mountain Jews (Juhuro) of the Caucasus (Azerbaijan, Dagestan), and the Georgian Jews (Gruzinim) of Georgia. Three languages (Bukhori, Judeo-Tat, Kivruli), three cuisines (plov, khinkali, harissa), three musical traditions (Shashmaqom, lezginka, polyphony) — and one common Soviet trauma followed by mass aliyah after 1989. Together: roughly 300,000 Israelis. This synthesis lesson reviews the core terms from lessons 381 through 389.

Cultural note — These three groups are NOT interchangeable, and conflating them is a common mistake. Bukharan = Persian-speaking, Central Asian, Sephardic-rite, Silk Road merchants. Mountain Jews = Tat-speaking, Caucasian, with their own Juhuro identity, often warrior-coded. Georgian = Kartvelian-speaking, Caucasian Christian-adjacent, philosemitic host culture, oldest of the three (~2600 years). Learn one neighborhood marker for each and you will navigate Israeli social geography correctly.
