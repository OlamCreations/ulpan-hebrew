Root ב־ט־ל — nullifying, idleness

Roots Atlas 214 · שֹׁרֶשׁ ב ט ל · the cancelling-and-idleness root
Home ב ט ל Morphology
ב ט ל
v · t · l
everything to do with making something void, and doing nothing
6 words · 1 root · 3 verb-voices

One idea sits at the centre: void, null, brought to nothing. To cancel is to make a thing void; a person who comes to nothing, who does nothing, is an idler; the state of having no work is unemployment. The root swings between two faces, an active "to cancel" and a passive/reflexive "to be idle, to come to naught". From there it builds the noun for cancellation, the loafer, and the social term for unemployment. Every word here is verified against pealim, niqqud included.

The verb across the binyanim (voices)

The same root runs through the verb system. Each binyan is a voice: it bends the one idea into active, passive, causative or reciprocal. Tap to hear.

Pa'al
לִבְטֹלlivtol
simple active — the plain action
to cease to exist, to become void (literary)
Pi'el
לְבַטֵּלlevatel
intensive / specialized active
to cancel, to annul, to undo
Hitpa'el
לְהִתְבַּטֵּלlehitbatel
reflexive / reciprocal — to / on oneself
to idle, to laze around, to come to nothing

The pi'el levatel is the workhorse, "to cancel" something actively; the hitpa'el lehitbatel turns it inward, to be idle or come to nothing. The pa'al livtol is mostly literary. The passives pu'al and huf'al and the hif'il are not lexicalized here, which is why "to be cancelled" is carried by the pi'el/hitpa'el pair rather than a dedicated passive.
