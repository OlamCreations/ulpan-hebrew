Az Yashir — Then He Sang

HomeAz Yashir
Avraham Aviv Alush's "Az Yashir" — a contemporary Mizrahi-style setting of Shirat HaYam (the Song of the Sea, Exodus 15), released as part of the Shabbat MiKol HaSibot ("Sabbath for All Reasons") collective project. The collective brings together Israeli artists to record contemporary arrangements of traditional Shabbat-themed texts. Aviv Alush — an Israeli actor (he plays Yeshua in The Chosen) and musician of Moroccan-Israeli heritage — brings a warm Mizrahi/Maghrebi sensibility to the Hebrew text. The lyrics are direct biblical quotation from Exodus 15: the song Moses and the Israelites sang on the far shore of the Red Sea, watching the Egyptian army vanish into the waves. This page teaches that biblical Hebrew — listen to Aviv Alush's recording on YouTube for the melody and the full bilingual arrangement.

♫ Singing notes

  • Mizrahi-Israeli worship feel. A-minor key with a Phrygian/freygish lift — the E chord acts as V of Am, giving the cadence its Mizrahi Jewish-mode color. The progression I've put under the verses is a suggestion — listen to Aviv Alush's recording and adjust by ear, especially in V12 where he likely takes the "Mi chamocha" phrase higher.
  • Breath at the commas. Hebrew poetry uses balanced parallelism — V2 has the classic structure "horse and rider // He has cast into the sea". Each half-line gets its own breath. V12 is built on a triple repetition of the question "mi chamocha" — let each one ring before the next.
  • V1 is unusual. "Az yashir Moshe" uses the imperfect verb form (יָשִׁיר, future tense) to describe a past action. The rabbis (Mechilta) read this grammatical oddity as meaning Moses sang and will sing — the song belongs to every generation, sung again whenever God delivers. The az ("then") fixes a moment that keeps recurring.
  • The iconic Mi Chamocha. Exodus 15:11 is sung in every Shacharit (morning) service, every Maariv (evening) service, and at the heart of the Pesach Seder. It is the most-repeated single biblical line in Jewish liturgy after Shema Yisrael. Learning V12 here gives you a line you'll hear daily once you start davening.
  • Pronunciation watch:
    • gao gaa (15:1) — infinitive absolute + perfect of the same root, an emphatic biblical construction. Translate "He has utterly triumphed". The two words are nearly identical — same letters in different vocalization.
    • ozzi v'zimrat Yah (15:2) — note the doubled zayin in ozzi (my strength). The short divine name Yah (יָהּ) is used here for poetic compactness, not the long Tetragrammaton.
    • va-aromemenhu (15:2) — long word, single breath. From the root ר.ו.ם (to be high), polel form. "I will exalt Him".
    • chamocha / kamocha (15:11) — same word, slight phonetic variation (kaf with/without dagesh). "Like You". The chaf sound (כ without dagesh) is a soft guttural; the kaf with dagesh is a hard "k".
  • The strum / drone. For Mizrahi feel, let the Am drone underneath while the melody floats on top. Capo 2 for B minor brightness, or capo 5 for D minor if you want the deeper Mizrahi resonance.

♦ About this song

A modern Mizrahi-flavored setting of Shirat HaYam (the Song of the Sea, Exodus 15) by Israeli actor-singer Avraham Aviv Alush, released as part of the Shabbat MiKol HaSibot collective project. The Hebrew lyrics are direct biblical quotation — the first song in the Torah, sung at the Red Sea, recited every morning in synagogue worldwide.

Avraham Aviv Alush — who he is

Avraham Aviv Alush (אברהם אביב אלוש) is an Israeli actor and musician of Moroccan-Israeli heritage, born in Be'er Sheva in 1982. He came to international attention for playing Yeshua (Jesus) in the long-running streaming drama The Chosen — the first Israeli actor cast in that role in a major American production. Alongside acting, he records and performs Mizrahi-inflected Hebrew music, often weaving liturgical and biblical texts into contemporary arrangements. His vocal style is warm, mid-range, with the Sephardi pronunciation that has become the standard in Israeli Hebrew.

Shabbat MiKol HaSibot — the project

Shabbat MiKol HaSibot (שבת מכל הסיבות — literally "Sabbath, from all reasons" / idiomatically "Sabbath for every reason in the world") is a collaborative Israeli music project that brings together multiple artists to record contemporary arrangements of Shabbat-themed Hebrew texts — psalms, zemirot, biblical poetry, and original compositions tied to the rhythm of the Jewish week. Aviv Alush's "Az Yashir" was released under this project, alongside friends and other Israeli musicians.

The project reflects a broader trend in contemporary Israeli music: the return of religious vocabulary to mainstream pop, by artists who would not previously have been classified as religious. Hebrew liturgy is being re-sung by secular and traditional Israelis alike, often in Mizrahi-fusion styles that mix Maghrebi, Yemenite, and Andalusian sensibilities with Western pop production.

Shirat HaYam — the biblical source

Exodus 15:1-18 is the Song of the Sea, sung by Moses and the Israelites after crossing the Red Sea on dry ground. In the Torah scroll, Shirat HaYam is written in a unique brick-pattern layout (אריח על גבי אריח) — the words arranged like a wall of bricks, evoking either the walls of water that stood up on either side, or the structured architecture of poetry itself.

The song is recited every morning in Jewish liturgy, in the Pesukei d'Zimra (verses of song) section of Shacharit. It is read aloud from the Torah on the seventh day of Pesach — the traditional anniversary of the splitting of the sea. Many congregations rise to their feet for this reading.

The song falls into three movements:

  • 1-3: God as warrior who saves (the part this song quotes most).
  • 4-12: the narrative of Pharaoh's destruction — descriptive, dramatic, with the famous lines about the sea covering them.
  • 13-18: the future — God leading the people to His holy mountain, and the closing declaration "Adonai yimloch l'olam va'ed" ("The LORD will reign forever and ever") which becomes the final line of every Jewish morning service.

Mi Chamocha — verse 4 in detail

Exodus 15:11 is the theological climax of Shirat HaYam. The question form — "Mi chamocha?" ("Who is like You?") — is rhetorical: the expected answer is "no one". This verse becomes a permanent liturgical building block. In every morning service the cantor sings "Mi chamocha ba'eilim Adonai" just before the Amidah, and the congregation echoes it. The same line appears in the evening service. It is the most-recited biblical verse in Jewish daily prayer after Shema Yisrael.

Vocabulary worth committing to memory:

  • נֶאְדָּר (ne'edar) — "glorious / majestic". Niphal participle of א.ד.ר (to be wide / majestic). Same root as aderet (cloak / mantle, as in Elijah's mantle).
  • נוֹרָא (nora) — "awesome / fearsome / inspiring fear". Niphal participle of י.ר.א (to fear). Same root as yirah (awe), the standard Hebrew word for religious awe. Modern Hebrew slang preserves nora as the all-purpose intensifier ("very", "incredibly" — nora yafe, "incredibly beautiful").
  • פֶלֶא (pele) — "wonder / miracle". Same root as nifla'ot (wonders). One of the names of the Messiah in Isaiah 9:6 (Pele Yo'etz).
  • תְהִלֹּת (tehilot) — "praises", plural of tehillah. The book of Psalms is called Tehillim (Praises) from the same root.

How to learn this song

1. Start with V12 (Mi chamocha) — it's the shortest, the most musically distinctive, and you will encounter it again every single morning of your life once you're davening.

2. V3 (ozzi v'zimrat Yah... Elohei avi va-aromemenhu) appears in many liturgical settings and bar/bat mitzvah ceremonies. Useful beyond this song.

3. V1 (the framing "Then Moses sang...") gives you the verb pattern of biblical Hebrew narration — particularly the imperfect-for-past quirk that is the rabbis' favorite Talmudic prooftext.

4. Listen to Aviv Alush's recording several times before trying to sing along — his Mizrahi melisma (the small vocal turns and ornaments) is the hardest part to imitate, much more than the words. Don't worry about getting it perfect.
