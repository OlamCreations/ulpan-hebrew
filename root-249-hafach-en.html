Root ה־פ־ך: turning over

Roots Atlas 249 · שֹׁרֶשׁ ה פ ך · the turning-over root
Home ה פ ך Morphology
ה פ ך
h · p · kh
everything to do with flipping, reversing, and becoming the opposite
7 words · 1 root · 1 verb-voices

One gesture runs through this whole family: turning something over. Flip a thing and it faces the other way, so the noun הֶפֶךְ is the opposite, and the adjective הָפוּךְ is upside-down, reversed, the very word in קָפֶה הָפוּךְ, a "flipped coffee", a latte. Flip a state of affairs and you get a reversal; flip a whole society and you get a revolution, מַהְפֵּכָה. The plain verb even does grammar duty: "to turn into" something. From a coffee order to the overthrow of a regime, it is the same three letters turning over. Every word is verified against pealim, niqqud included.

The verb across the binyanim (voices)

The same root runs through the verb system. Each binyan is a voice: it bends the one idea into active, passive, causative or reciprocal. Tap to hear.

Pa'al
לַהֲפֹךְlahafokh
simple active, the plain action
to turn over, to flip; to turn into, to become (ל-)

Only the simple pa'al is lexicalized for everyday use, but it is remarkably elastic: "to flip" a pancake and "to turn into" something are the same verb. The reversing meaning is then carried mostly by adjectives (הָפוּךְ, הָפִיךְ) and a rich set of nouns rather than by extra binyanim.

Words born from the root

Same root letters, different noun moulds (mishkalim). The mould tells you the function: who does it, the act of doing it, or the thing produced.
