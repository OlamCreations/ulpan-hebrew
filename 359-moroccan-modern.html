Modern Moroccan-Israeli Community

Today's Moroccan-Israeli community is the largest single-origin Mizrahi group in Israel — roughly one million people of Moroccan descent, concentrated in the development towns of the south and the port cities. After decades of marginalization, the community fought for and won its place: Black Panthers in 1971, an official Heritage Day for Jews of Arab Lands in 2014, the Hilula of Baba Sali drawing 250,000 yearly to Netivot, and since the 2020 Abraham Accords, direct flights reconnecting Casablanca and Marrakech to Tel Aviv. This lesson maps the contemporary reality.

Cultural note — When you arrive at Ben Gurion and ride south, the development towns you pass through (Yeruham, Dimona, Ofakim, Netivot, Sderot) are where Moroccan Jews were settled in the 1950s, often by force, in tents (ma'abarot) on the desert frontier. Sixty years later these same towns produced ministers, generals, philosophers, and the political reshaping of Israel. The Mizrahi story is not a side story — it is half the country. Speak some Moroccan Hebrew and you unlock half the conversations in Ashdod, Beersheva, and the Negev.
