Judeo-Iraqi Arabic

Lesson 337 · עֲרָבִית יְהוּדִית-עִירָאקִית
Home337

For nearly 2,500 years, Iraqi Jews spoke a distinct Arabic dialect — qeltu — that set them apart from their Muslim neighbors who spoke gilit. The two communities lived in the same Baghdad streets but said "I said" in different ways: a Jew said "qeltu", a Muslim said "gilit". This single phonetic divide opens a whole world: a Hebrew-laced commerce language, a literary tradition (Anwar Sha'ul, Mir Basri), a liturgical role parallel to Ladino, and a heritage now preserved at the Babylonian Jewry Heritage Center in Or Yehuda. Today's Iraqi-Israeli families still code-switch between Hebrew and remnants of qeltu at the Shabbat table.

Cultural note — The qeltu/gilit divide is one of the cleanest dialect splits in the Arab world. Qeltu was spoken by the urban Jewish, Christian, and older Muslim population of Baghdad, Mosul, Basra, and northern Iraqi cities — a sedentary dialect descended from medieval Iraqi Arabic. Gilit arrived later with Bedouin migrations and became the dominant Muslim Baghdadi dialect. By the 20th century, qeltu had become almost exclusively Jewish (and Christian) in Baghdad. When 130,000 Iraqi Jews left in 1950-51 (Operation Ezra and Nehemiah), the dialect emigrated with them. Today it survives in Or Yehuda, Ramat Gan, and a shrinking number of elderly speakers.
