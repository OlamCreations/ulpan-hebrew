Bukharan & Caucasian Jewish Food Culture

Lesson 385 · אוֹכֶל בּוּכָרִי וְקַוְקָזִי
Home385

Central Asian and Caucasus Jewish cuisine arrived in Israel with the great Bukharan, Mountain Jewish (Juhuro), and Georgian aliyot — bringing plov, khinkali, khachapuri and a tea-and-rice culture that reshaped neighborhoods like Bukharim quarter in Jerusalem and Hadar in Haifa. This lesson covers the signature dishes, the Shabbat overnight specialties, and the social rituals (right-hand plov, green tea ceremony) that distinguish these communities from both Ashkenazi and Mizrahi mainstreams.

Cultural note — Bukharan Jews kept strict kashrut for centuries in Muslim Central Asia, which gave them a distinctive food signature: zero pork ever (unlike surrounding Russian/Soviet cuisine), heavy lamb and beef, rice as staple, and a Shabbat overnight stew (oshi sabo) that predates and parallels the Ashkenazi cholent. Mountain Jews of the Caucasus share the rice culture but add Georgian-influenced walnut sauces and dumplings. In Israel today these cuisines fuse with Mizrahi and Yemenite traditions in restaurants like Bukhara on Rechov Yehezkel in Jerusalem.
