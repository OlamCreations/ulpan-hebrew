Lesson 411 · תּוֹלְדוֹת הַתֵּאַטְרוֹן הַיִּשְׂרְאֵלִי

Israeli theatre did not begin in Tel Aviv. It began in Moscow in 1917, in the basement of a former synagogue, when a young Vilna teacher named Nahum Tzemach gathered actors to perform in a language no theatre had ever used: modern Hebrew. From that revolutionary cell came Habima, the most improbable national theatre in history — a Russian troupe that crossed Europe, played in Berlin and Vienna and New York, and finally settled in Eretz Yisrael in 1928. This lesson traces the four pillars: Habima, Ohel, Cameri, and the Yiddish theatre tradition they all argued with.

Cultural note — The choice of Hebrew over Yiddish in early 20th-century theatre was not aesthetic. It was ideological warfare. Yiddish was spoken by millions; Hebrew was the language of prayer, dead in daily speech for 1,800 years. Putting Hebrew on a stage was an act of resurrection, and it split the Jewish world. Habima made the Hebrew choice in Moscow before Israel existed; Cameri rebelled against Habima\'s grandiose style 27 years later. Israeli theatre carries both lineages.
