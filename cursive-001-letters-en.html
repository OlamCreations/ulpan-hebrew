Israelis do not handprint. The block letters you have learned (כְּתָב דְּפוּס, print) are for screens and books. By hand, a shopping list, a note on the fridge, a teacher at the whiteboard, a form you fill at the Misrad HaPnim, everyone writes in cursive (כְּתָב יָד, "hand script"). If you cannot read it, half of daily Hebrew stays closed to you. This lesson maps every print letter to its cursive twin. Same letter, same sound, different shape.

No stroke-order rules, Unlike Chinese or Japanese, Hebrew has no fixed stroke order, in print or cursive. There is a common way most people form each letter, but it varies person to person. The shapes below are the standard Ashkenazi cursive taught in Israeli schools. To write: copy the shape on paper, find a motion that feels natural, repeat.
The font is real handwriting, Every cursive letter on this page is rendered in KtavYadCLM, an actual Israeli handwriting typeface. What you see is what a neat Israeli hand looks like. Tap any card to hear the letter.

5 Final Forms (sofit): Print and Cursive

Final forms drop below the line, In cursive the sofit letters (ך ם ן ף ץ) are even clearer than in print: most send a long tail under the baseline. When you see a stroke dropping low at the end of a word, it is almost always a final letter.
