Ladino — Judeo-Spanish

Lesson 375 · לָאדִינוֹ — סְפָרַדִית יְהוּדִית
Home375

Ladino (also called Judezmo, Espanyol, or Judeo-Spanish) is the language Sephardi Jews carried out of Iberia after the 1492 expulsion. It is essentially fifteenth-century Castilian and Aragonese, frozen at the moment of exile, then enriched across five centuries with Hebrew, Aramaic, Turkish, Greek, Arabic, and Italian loans gathered along the Mediterranean diaspora. Written for centuries in Hebrew script (Rashi for print, Solitreo for cursive), Ladino survived in Salonika, Constantinople, Sarajevo, Sofia, Izmir, and the Balkan-Anatolian arc until the Holocaust devastated its speaker base in 1943-44. Today roughly 100,000 speakers remain, mostly elderly. This lesson maps the language, its loanwords, its songbook, its proverbs, and the modern revival.

Cultural note — When a Sephardi grandmother in Jerusalem says "kale saver" (one must know) or sings "Adio kerida," she is speaking the Spanish of Cervantes' grandparents, kept alive by exile. Ladino is not a dialect of modern Spanish — it diverged in 1492 and never met the Real Academia. Ashkenazi Jews preserved Yiddish; Sephardim preserved this. The Autoridad Nasionala del Ladino in Israel (founded 1996) and the Ladino Komite are now the institutional guardians. If you meet an older Israeli of Salonika, Sarajevo, or Sofia descent, "Ke haber?" (how are you, from Turkish) opens a door.
