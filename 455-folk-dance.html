Israeli Folk Dance — Rikudei Am

Lesson 455 · רִקּוּדֵי עַם
Home455

Israeli folk dance — rikudei am — is a deliberately invented tradition. In 1944 Gurit Kadman organized the first Dalia Festival at Kibbutz Dalia, gathering kibbutz dance leaders to forge a shared movement vocabulary from Yemenite, Hassidic, Russian-folk, Romanian-Hora, and Druze sources. Eighty years later, weekly harkadot (folk-dance evenings) run in roughly 200 towns and on 5 continents. This lesson covers the founding, the canonical dances (Hora Agadati, Mayim Mayim, Tzadik Katamar, Erev Ba), the harkadah ritual, and the modern competition scene.

Cultural note — A weekly harkadah is part synagogue, part nightclub. People show up Tuesday at 8 pm, dance four hours, and are home by midnight. The leader (rakad) plays recorded music, calls the dance, demonstrates if needed, and rotates the playlist between classics ("Mayim Mayim") and current Israeli pop adaptations. Many secular Israelis got married to a partner first met at a harkadah.
