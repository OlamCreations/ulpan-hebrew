Tehilim 8: How majestic is Your name

תְּהִלִּים ח · יְהֹוָה אֲדֹנֵינוּ
HomePs 8
A creation hymn framed as an inclusio: the same line opens and closes the psalm (v.2 and v.10), how majestic is Your name in all the earth. Between the two, a single vertiginous question, when I look at the heavens, what is a frail human being that You should care (v.5), answered by a coronation: man set a little less than the divine, given dominion over the works of creation. Recited at the New Moon and on the festivals tied to renewal.

The inclusio closes. Word for word the opening of v.2 returns, but without its second stich (no "above the heavens"). After the descent through all creation, the eye comes back to rest on the Name alone. The repetition is not redundancy: it is the same line, now filled by everything seen between the two.

♦ PARDES analysis: פרדס

The four levels of Jewish reading of the sacred text: פשט Peshat (literal meaning), רמז Remez (symbolic allusion), דרש Drash (midrashic interpretation), סוד Sod (mystical meaning). The word פרדס means "orchard", each level is a fruit.

פשט · Peshat: literal meaning

V.1: a superscription, for the leader, on the gittith, a psalm of David, a performance note, not part of the prayer itself. V.2: the body opens by addressing God twice, YHWH our Lord, and marvels that His name fills all the earth while His splendor is set above the heavens. V.3: even the babble of infants founds a stronghold against His foes. V.4-5: looking up at the moon and stars, the poet asks the great question, what is man that You remember him? V.6-9: the answer, God made man little less than the divine, crowned with glory, ruler over flocks, beasts, birds, and fish. V.10: the opening line returns, word for word, sealing the whole.

רמז · Remez: allusion

הַגִּתִּית (ha-gittit, v.1) is an enigma the JPS itself flags as uncertain: perhaps a tune "of Gath," perhaps a wine-press instrument (from gat, the winepress). The veil over the very first word warns the reader, this song will reach beyond what can be named. אֱנוֹשׁ (enosh, v.5) is not the strong adam or ish but the frail, mortal human, the same root as anush, "incurably sick", the most fragile word for man set against the immensity of shamayim, the heavens (v.4). The greatness of the question lives in that gap.

דרש · Drash: interpretation

מְּעַט מֵאֱלֹהִים (me'at me-elohim, v.6) = "a little less than the divine." JPS itself notes the older reading "the angels", the rabbis read the dignity of man as set just one rung below the heavenly host, not below the beasts. The verb וַתְּחַסְּרֵהוּ (va-techasrehu, v.6), "You made him lack," frames that nearness as a deliberate, measured withholding: man is great precisely because he is held back from the full, a creature on the threshold. The inclusio (v.2 = v.10) teaches that all human dominion (v.6-9) is bracketed by, and answerable to, the one majestic Name, power is on loan, never owned.

סוד · Sod: mystical meaning

The psalm is a vertical ladder. V.3, the mouths of infants found oz (strength), the purest, most unformed speech upholds creation, as the Zohar reads the breath of schoolchildren sustaining the world. The arc descends from the heavens (v.4) down to the fish in the depths of the sea (v.9), and man stands at the hinge: me'at me-elohim (v.6), the meeting point of upper and lower worlds, the being through whom the divine Name becomes majestic in all the earth. The closing inclusio (v.10) is not a repetition but a return on a higher turn of the spiral: the same words, now filled by everything seen between them. To say this psalm is to climb from the babble of a child to the crown of kavod and back, and find the Name unchanged at both ends.
