Mohammed V & WWII Protection

Lesson 352 · מוּחַמַּד הַחֲמִישִׁי וְהַהַגַּנַת יְהוּדִים בְּמִלְחֶמֶת הָעוֹלָם
Home352

In June 1940 France fell. The Vichy regime extended its anti-Jewish statutes (Statut des Juifs) across the protectorate of French Morocco. ~250,000 Moroccan Jews suddenly faced legal exclusion, professional bans, and the threat of deportation. Then something remarkable happened. Sultan Mohammed V — technically a French puppet — refused. Not in a fiery speech but in cold administrative resistance. He refused to deliver lists. He refused to enforce yellow stars. He famously told Vichy officials his Jewish subjects were "Moroccans, not Jews." The story is contested, romanticized, and historically complex — but the protection was real, and ~250K Jews survived where French Jews next door did not.

Cultural note — The Mohammed V story matters in modern Israel-Morocco relations. King Mohammed VI (the current king, grandson of Mohammed V) made the WWII protection narrative central to the 2020 Abraham Accords normalization with Israel. Yad Vashem has debated the "Righteous Among the Nations" status for decades — Mohammed V had limited real power under Vichy, and the protection was administrative rather than physical rescue. But the symbolic weight is enormous: a Muslim king who refused complicity. Today Casablanca preserves the largest Jewish community in the Arab world (~2,000 people), and Moroccan-Israeli ties run deeper than any other Arab country. Your Hebrew opens this conversation.
