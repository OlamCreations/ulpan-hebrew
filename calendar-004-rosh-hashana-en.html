Rosh HaShana: The Day of Judgment

רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה · 1-2 TISHREI · SEPT-OCT · YOM HARAT OLAM · YOM TERUAH
Home Rosh HaShana
Rosh HaShana ("head of the year") is the Jewish civil new year and the first of the Yamim Noraim (Days of Awe). Falls on 1-2 Tishrei (two consecutive days, observed even in Israel, uniquely among festivals). Traditionally the day of judgment, God writes each person in the Book of Life or the Book of Death, sealed on Yom Kippur 10 days later. Central act: blowing the shofar (100 blasts each day). Central foods: apple dipped in honey (for a sweet year), round challah (cycle of the year), pomegranate (613 seeds = 613 mitzvot, according to tradition), fish head (rosh = head). Greeting: Shana tova! (Good year) or L'shana tova tikatevu (May you be inscribed for a good year).

The Torah doesn't name the holiday Rosh HaShana, that's a later rabbinic name. The Torah calls it Zichron Teruah (Lev 23:24) or Yom Teruah (Num 29:1), the day of the shofar blast. The 'remembering' (zikaron) is mutual: God remembers humanity, humanity remembers God's judgment.

The second Torah passage about Rosh HaShana, in Numbers. Together with Lev 23:24, this is the biblical bedrock. Note: no mention of 'new year', the rabbis derived that from the cycle of months and from the tradition that Adam was created on 1 Tishrei.

♦ About Rosh HaShana

Two days of synagogue (4-6 hours each morning), shofar blowing, festive meals, judgment imagery, sweetness symbolism. The opening of the 'Ten Days of Repentance' (Aseret Yemei Teshuva) that closes with Yom Kippur. In Israel: most stores closed for two days, public transport halted, beaches packed the day before.

The shofar

Made from a ram's horn, recalling the binding of Isaac (Akedah), where a ram was sacrificed in his place (Gen 22:13). 100 blasts total each day, in 4 sets (with the patterns Tekiah / Shevarim-Teruah / Tekiah, Tekiah / Shevarim / Tekiah, Tekiah / Teruah / Tekiah). Each set ends with a long tekiah gedola. Cannot be blown when Rosh HaShana falls on Shabbat (in Israel about 30% of years).

Tashlich

On the afternoon of the first day, Jews walk to a body of water (river, lake, sea, even a fish pond) and symbolically 'cast' their sins into the water, based on Micah 7:19 (v'tashlich bi'metzulot yam kol chatotam, 'and You will cast all their sins into the depths of the sea'). In Tel Aviv: huge gatherings on the beaches. In Jerusalem: at the few small streams or fountains in the city, often combined with picnic-like atmosphere.

The seder of foods

Beyond apple-and-honey, many communities (especially Sephardic) hold a Rosh HaShana seder with symbolic foods: karti (leeks, yikartu 'cut off' our enemies), silka (beet greens, sheyistalku), tamri (dates, shey'itamu, end of evil), rubia (black-eyed peas), kerati (gourd), rosh kevess (sheep head, 'may we be at the head'), rimon (pomegranate).

The judgment frame

Mishnah Rosh HaShana 1:2: 'On Rosh HaShana all who come into the world pass before Him like sheep before a shepherd.' Each person is judged for the coming year. The liturgy is full of melech (king) imagery, God enthroned, books opened, deeds weighed. Yet the holiday is also festive: white tablecloths, fine clothes, joyful food. The paradox: judgment is joyful when you trust the Judge.

Modern Israel

Major civic holiday. Schools closed ~3 weeks (the 'tishrei holidays' bloc, Rosh HaShana → Yom Kippur → Sukkot → Simchat Torah). El Al and many other businesses run reduced schedules. Tel Aviv 'restaurant scene' often closes for the two days, reopens day 3. Rosh HaShana cards (paper or digital) sent to family and business contacts, phrase: Shana tova u'metukah (good and sweet year).
