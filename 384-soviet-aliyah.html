Soviet Aliyah Waves

Lesson 384 · עֲלִיַּת יְהוּדֵי בְּרִית הַמּוֹעָצוֹת
Home384

Between 1968 and 1991, the Iron Curtain cracked open enough to let roughly one million Jews leave the USSR. They did not leave as one people. Ashkenazi Russians from Moscow and Leningrad, Georgian Jews from Tbilisi and Kutaisi, Bukharan Jews from Samarkand and Tashkent, and Mountain Jews (Juhuro) from Derbent and Quba each moved on different timelines, took different routes (Vienna, then HIAS Rome, then Israel or Brooklyn), and clustered in different absorption towns. This lesson covers the four waves and the Hebrew you need to discuss them with an oleh of that generation.

Why this matters for your aliyah — If you settle in Or Yehuda, Bat Yam, Ramla, Ashdod, or southern Beer Sheva, your neighbors will include grandchildren of those waves. Many synagogues still hold services in Bukhori, Juhuri, or Georgian Hebrew nusach. Knowing the timeline (Refusenikim, Helsinki, Jackson-Vanik, 1989 collapse) is basic Israeli civic literacy — the same way a French oleh knows about the Algerian Aliyah of 1962.
