Russian Jews — FSU Aliyah Wave

Lesson 273 · יְהוּדִים מִבְּרִית הַמּוֹעָצוֹת לְשֶׁעָבַר
Home273

The 1990s FSU Aliyah was the largest single Jewish migration in Israeli history: roughly 1 million Russian-speakers came between 1989 and 2003 from Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, Central Asia, and the Caucasus. It reshaped Israeli demographics — Russian-speakers and their children are about 17% of the population today. Major centers include Bat Yam, Ashdod, Beit Shemesh, Be\'er Sheva, Petach Tikva, Ariel, Karmiel, and Haifa. Three generations now coexist: the 1st generation (60+), the 2nd (35-50), and the 3rd born in Israel — with a "1.5 generation" of children who arrived young. Politically, the community\'s most visible voice is Avigdor Lieberman\'s Yisrael Beiteinu party.

Cultural note — The 1990s Russian aliyah brought roughly 25% of Soviet Jewry — an extraordinary brain drain of engineers, doctors, musicians, scientists. The orchestra-engineer-turned-taxi-driver cliché is real but oversimplified: many requalified, others didn\'t. Russian-Israeli identity is layered: many families keep strong Russian at home ("kitchen Russian") while children speak Hebrew at school. Yisrael Beiteinu represents secular-nationalist sentiment within the community. Cultural exports include Russian-language Israeli cinema, HaKochav HaBa winners, pop singers, and ballet stars — Russian-Israeli culture is now a permanent layer of Israeli mainstream.
