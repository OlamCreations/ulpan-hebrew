Israeli Cinema — Early History

Lesson 401 · קוֹלְנוֹעַ יִשְׂרְאֵלִי מֻקְדָּם
Home401

Israeli cinema is younger than the State itself only by a few decades — Yaakov Ben-Dov was already filming the Yishuv in 1917, three decades before independence. From silent Zionist newsreels to Bourekas comedies that mocked the Ashkenazi-Mizrachi divide, from "Hill 24 Doesn't Answer" (the first Israeli feature shown at Cannes) to Uri Zohar shattering every convention with "Hole in the Moon", Israeli film grew up in public, on a tight budget, with a culture arguing about itself. This lesson maps the foundational era — pioneers, heroic-pioneer cinema, Bourekas, the New Sensibility, and the industrialization through Cannon Films and the Israel Film Fund.

Cultural note — In Israel, "Bourekas films" (סִרְטֵי בּוּרֶקָס) is named after the Sephardic pastry: cheap, popular, made for the masses. Critics used the word as an insult; audiences turned it into a badge. The genre was the first Israeli cinema where Mizrachi characters were heroes, not extras. When Sallah Shabati (1964) was nominated for an Oscar, Ephraim Kishon — Hungarian-born, writing in Hebrew about a Mizrachi immigrant — proved Israeli film could speak to the world without imitating it.
