Operation Magic Carpet

Lesson 343 · מִבְצָע עַל כַּנְפֵי נְשָׁרִים
Home343

June 1949 to September 1950. About 49,000 Yemenite Jews — nearly the entire community — airlifted from British-controlled Aden to the new State of Israel in roughly 380 flights. The official name was Mivtza Al Kanfei Nesharim ("On Wings of Eagles," from Exodus 19:4). The popular name, Operation Magic Carpet, was Western press romanticism. The reality was harder: a thousand-year community walking through hostile mountains, packed into transport planes with seats removed, then dropped into tin shacks in a country in austerity. This lesson covers the persecution that triggered it, the journey, the airlift logistics, and the painful aftermath that Israel still has not fully reckoned with.

Cultural note — For Yemenite Jews, Magic Carpet is not a triumphant story. It is a story of rescue, yes, but also of a community whose traditions were dismissed as primitive by Ashkenazi authorities, whose children were lost in the Yemenite Children Affair, and whose integration into Israel was rougher than the official narrative admits. When you meet Yemenite Israelis (Teimanim), the right posture is respect for a community that preserved Hebrew pronunciation, liturgy, and mystical tradition for two millennia in isolation — and skepticism toward the "rescue mission" framing that erases their agency.
