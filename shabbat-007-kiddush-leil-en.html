Kiddush Leil Shabbat: Friday Night Sanctification

קִדּוּשׁ לֵיל שַׁבָּת · Friday night before the meal · Vayechulu + wine + sanctification
HomeKiddush Leil
The Friday-night Kiddush, the formal sanctification of Shabbat over a cup of wine, said standing at the table before sitting down to the meal. Three parts: (1) the biblical preamble Vayechulu, Genesis 2:1-3 read aloud as a witness statement that God completed creation and sanctified the seventh day; (2) the wine blessing, Borei pri hagafen, the standard blessing for wine; (3) the Shabbat sanctification, Mekadesh haShabbat, a longer blessing that thanks God for choosing Israel, commanding the Sabbath, and giving it as an inheritance. Said by the head of household (traditionally the man, but customs vary), with everyone standing and listening, then drinking a sip of wine. Followed by Netilat Yadayim and HaMotzi.

Read Genesis 2:1-3 (Vayechulu) on Sefaria for the surrounding biblical/liturgical context.

♦ Lesson

  • When, Friday night at the start of the meal, after returning from synagogue. Must be over wine (or grape juice for those who don't drink alcohol), in a cup holding at least 86ml (a revi'it). The person saying Kiddush drinks the majority; others drink small sips.
  • How to say it, stand. Hold the cup in the right hand (or pass to right hand). Begin by quietly saying "Yom haShishi" (these two words come from Gen 1:31 and link the preamble to the creation narrative). Then aloud: Vayechulu (Gen 2:1-3), then the wine blessing, then the Shabbat blessing. Drink the cup. Hand it around for everyone to taste.
  • Vayechulu functions as a witness, the rabbis teach that whoever says the Vayechulu paragraph on Friday night is called "God's partner in the work of creation" (Talmud, Shabbat 119b). The reciter is testifying that the world was created in six days and rested on the seventh.
  • Vocabulary, three different words for "finished":
    • vayechulu (וַיְכֻלּוּ), "and they were finished/completed" (pual passive of כ.ל.ה). Both heavens and earth completed at once.
    • vayechal (וַיְכַל), "and He finished" (piel active of the same root). God completes the work.
    • vayikadesh (וַיְקַדֵּשׁ), "and He sanctified" (piel of ק.ד.ש). To sanctify is to make holy, i.e., to set apart from the ordinary.
  • The wine blessing, Borei Pri HaGafen: same blessing as for any wine. Borei is a qal participle ("Creator / one who creates") of ב.ר.א, the same root as bara at the start of Genesis ("In the beginning God created..."). Pri = fruit (construct of pri); hagafen = the vine (ha+gefen).
  • Rite differences:
    • Ashkenazi: full text including all three parts, recited audibly. Some communities add a brief preface mentioning Genesis 1:31's "Vayehi erev vayehi voker yom hashishi" silently before starting.
    • Sephardi / Mizrahi: slightly different phrasing in the Shabbat-sanctification paragraph (the structural meaning is identical). The wine blessing is the same.
    • Yemenite (Baladi): preserves an older liturgical text closer to the Geonic original; some phrases are simpler.

♦ About this prayer

The verbal moment that opens Shabbat at the table. A 60-second public declaration that God created the world, rested on the seventh day, and gave that rest to Israel, over a glass of wine.

Vayechulu: Genesis 2:1-3 as testimony

The Vayechulu paragraph is unique: it is one of the very few biblical passages that the rabbis instituted as a witness statement, meaning that reciting it on Friday night fulfills a halachic role parallel to giving courtroom testimony. The two witnesses to creation are the human community (saying Vayechulu) and the natural Shabbat itself (the day's distinct quality from the other six). Saying these three verses out loud is, technically, sanctifying the entire week's claim to a Creator.

The structure of the sanctification blessing

The third part, the Mekadesh haShabbat blessing, has six theological moves in ~50 words:

1. Sanctification: "who sanctified us with His commandments", the standard asher kid'shanu opening.
2. Election: "and was pleased with us", God chose Israel.
3. Inheritance: "and gave us His holy Sabbath as an inheritance", Shabbat is not earned, it's bequeathed.
4. Remembrance of creation: "a memorial of the work of creation", Shabbat as the weekly creation anniversary.
5. Remembrance of the Exodus: "a memorial of the going out from Egypt", Shabbat as a freedom-from-slavery declaration.
6. Closing: "Blessed are You, LORD, who sanctifies the Sabbath".

Creation + Exodus + present sanctification all in one paragraph. The Kiddush is theologically dense by design, every Friday night, every Jew is briefed on the cosmic premises of the next 25 hours.

The cup and the wine

The cup should be at least a revi'it (~86ml). Many homes use a dedicated silver or crystal Kiddush cup. The wine should fill it to overflowing slightly, the overflow is symbolic of overflowing blessing. Hold the cup in the right palm with four fingers underneath supporting and the thumb on the side.

The person who says Kiddush drinks at least the majority of the cup (a melo lugmav, "cheekful"); others take a small sip. Pouring extras from the Kiddush cup for everyone present is customary, especially for the children.

If wine is unavailable, Kiddush can be said over bread (challah) instead, in which case the wine blessing is replaced by HaMotzi, and the sequence shifts. But the standard form is over wine.
