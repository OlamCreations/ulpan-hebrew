Modern Hebrew poetry is one of the most surprising literary phenomena of the 20th century. A language slept for 1,800 years, then woke up — and within two generations produced poets translated into 40+ languages. Yehuda Amichai stands at the center: born in Würzburg, Germany, exiled to Palestine at 12, soldier in three wars, he wrote conversational poems where biblical Hebrew bumps against laundry, traffic, and grief. Ted Hughes called him "one of the half-dozen most important poets of the 20th century." This lesson maps the field around him.

Cultural note — Hebrew poetry sits in two simultaneous timeframes. When Amichai writes "my father", the word אָבִי carries Abraham, the priestly blessing, and the morning prayer — all at once. There is no neutral Hebrew. Every word is already a quote. This is why translation is hard: the reader of English loses the second voice underneath. When you read Hebrew poetry in Hebrew, you hear the Bible humming under the kitchen table.
