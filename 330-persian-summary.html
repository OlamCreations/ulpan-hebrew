Block 33 Summary — Persian Jewry

Lesson 330 · סִכּוּם בְּלוֹק 33 — יַהֲדוּת פָּרַס
Home330

Closing Block 33 with synthesis of one of the oldest continuous Jewish communities — 2700 years on Iranian soil, from Cyrus the Great (538 BCE) to the 1979 revolution and its aftermath. Persian Jewry shaped Purim, gave us Shushan, survived the Mashhadi forced conversion of 1839, prospered under Pahlavi, and saw its 100K population collapse to 8K after Khomeini. Today: ~150K Persian-Israelis (Katsav, Mofaz, Rita), a remnant in Tehran, and a tense Iran-Israel geopolitics.

Cultural note — Persian Jewry occupies a unique place: it predates Ashkenazi and most Sephardi communities by over a millennium. Esther and Mordechai walked Shushan; the Jewish presence in Iran is older than Islam itself. Iranian Jews never spoke Arabic, never wrote in Ladino — they spoke Judeo-Persian and contributed to Persian poetry, music (dastgah modes), and cuisine (gondi, tahdig, ghormeh sabzi). When Israelis speak of "Mizrahim," Persian Jews are a distinct sub-identity with their own synagogues, their own rite (Nusach Edot HaMizrach with Persian variations), and a memory of Shushan that no other diaspora carries.
